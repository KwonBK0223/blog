> **Paper**: SurvKAN: A Fully Parametric Survival Model Based on Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks — M. Mastroleo et al., 2026. [arXiv:2602.02179](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.02179)

## TL;DR

SurvKAN is a fully parametric, time-continuous survival model that feeds **time itself as an input** to a Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN), alongside the patient covariates, and predicts the log-hazard $\log h(t \mid x)$ directly. Instead of the Cox partial likelihood, it optimizes the full survival likelihood end-to-end, so there is no baseline hazard to eliminate and, as a consequence, no proportional hazards assumption to obey. It inherits the KAN-based interpretability machinery from CoxKAN (inspecting edge functions, pruning, symbolic conversion) while additionally modeling settings in which the hazard ratio between two patients changes over time.

## Background

Survival analysis has traditionally focused on negative events such as death, disease progression, and metastasis. Interest has recently broadened toward positive events as well (recovery, remission, hospital discharge), and beyond the clinic the same machinery is applied to reliability engineering, customer churn prediction, lifetime value estimation, and e-commerce conversion timing. In every case the core questions are the same: when does the event occur, and how does the risk of it occurring evolve over time?

### Two constraints of the Cox family

The Cox proportional hazards model factorizes the hazard into a baseline hazard and a patient-specific risk term.

$$
h(t \mid x) = h_0(t) \exp\big(\theta(x)\big)
$$

This structure carries two strong constraints simultaneously.

- **Linearity.** Classical CoxPH sets $\theta(x) = \beta^\top x$, so it cannot express nonlinear covariate effects or interactions between variables.
- **Proportional hazards.** For two patients $x_1$ and $x_2$, the hazard ratio

$$
\frac{h(t \mid x_1)}{h(t \mid x_2)} = \exp\big(\theta(x_1) - \theta(x_2)\big)
$$

does not depend on $t$. In other words, the relative ordering of "who is at higher risk" can never flip over time. Situations in which a treatment effect appears early and then fades, or in which risk spikes immediately after surgery and subsides over the long run, simply cannot be represented under this assumption.

### Deep learning and the black-box problem

DeepSurv and Cox-nnet replace $\theta(x)$ with a neural network, releasing the linearity constraint. DeepHit goes further, predicting event probabilities directly on a discrete time grid and thereby relaxing the PH assumption as well. Expressiveness is gained, but the resulting model is a black box. In clinical deployment the principle of "trust only what can be understood" still carries weight, and this lack of interpretability is a genuine bottleneck to adoption.

### What CoxKAN left open

CoxKAN predicts $\theta(x)$ with a KAN, a network whose edges carry learnable activation functions (B-splines), and then extracts a human-readable hazard formula through pruning and symbolic fitting. It secures interpretability and nonlinear expressiveness at once, but it remains **inside the semi-parametric Cox framework**.

It is worth being precise about what "semi-parametric" means here, because it is not a statement about optimization. CoxKAN is also trained end-to-end by backpropagation. Semi-parametric means that the baseline hazard $h_0(t)$ is never modeled explicitly; it is eliminated through the partial likelihood. The price of that choice is that CoxKAN has no internal handle on the time axis and remains bound to the proportional hazards assumption.

SurvKAN starts precisely here. Rather than eliminating the baseline hazard, it pulls time into the model as an input and represents the entire hazard parametrically.

## Method

### Architecture: covariates + time → log-hazard

Almost the entire difference between the two models is visible in their input/output definitions.

| | Input | Output | Handling of time |
|---|---|---|---|
| CoxKAN | covariates $x$ | log-partial hazard $\theta(x)$ | baseline hazard eliminated via partial likelihood |
| SurvKAN | covariates $x$ + time $t$ | log-hazard $\log h(t \mid x)$ | time dependence learned by the model |

The SurvKAN network takes $(x, t)$ and emits the log-hazard directly.

$$
\log h(t \mid x) = \text{KAN}(x, t)
$$

Because there is no multiplicative decomposition into a baseline hazard and a risk score, the hazard ratio

$$
\frac{h(t \mid x_1)}{h(t \mid x_2)}
$$

is now free to be a function of $t$. The **proportional hazards assumption is removed structurally.** The model learns directly how a given patient's risk rises and falls over time, and it can represent non-proportional settings in which the relative risk of two patients reverses at some point along the follow-up.

### Training: the full survival likelihood

SurvKAN optimizes the full survival likelihood rather than the Cox partial likelihood. For observations $(x_i, t_i, \delta_i)$, the likelihood is

$$
L = \prod_{i} h(t_i \mid x_i)^{\delta_i} \, S(t_i \mid x_i)
$$

where the survival function is recovered from the cumulative hazard.

$$
S(t \mid x) = \exp\left(-\int_0^t h(u \mid x)\, du\right)
$$

Taking logs gives the negative log-likelihood.

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{nll}} = -\sum_i \left[ \delta_i \log h(t_i \mid x_i) - \int_0^{t_i} h(u \mid x_i)\, du \right]
$$

The two terms have an intuitive division of labor.

- **Patients with an observed event ($\delta_i = 1$)**: the model is pushed to make the hazard $h(t_i \mid x_i)$ large at the event time.
- **Censored patients ($\delta_i = 0$)**: the model is pushed to keep the cumulative hazard up to $t_i$ small, i.e. to maintain a high survival probability up to the last observation.

Whereas the partial likelihood only learns relative orderings within risk sets, the full likelihood fits the entire survival curve directly.

### Numerical integration of the cumulative hazard

The integral $\int_0^{t} h(u \mid x)\, du$ has no closed form. SurvKAN discretizes time onto a grid, evaluates the KAN at each grid point, and approximates the cumulative hazard by numerical integration. The operation is differentiable, so gradients flow back through it into the KAN and the model trains end-to-end.

### Regularization

The final objective adds a KAN regularization term to the negative log-likelihood.

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{nll}} + \lambda R
$$

$R$ combines sparsity (L1 norm of the activations), smoothness (control of the spline coefficients), and entropy (concentrating usage on a few important edges). Its purpose is not only generalization but the induction of a **prunable KAN**: the interpretation stage only works if what remains after training is a structure that can actually be cut down.

### Inference and interpretability

At inference time, a patient's features are evaluated at a range of time points, the hazards are accumulated, and the survival curve is reconstructed. Because the network can be evaluated at any $t$, the model is time-continuous.

Interpretation proceeds by inspecting the KAN's edge functions directly.

- **Time edges** show how risk evolves as time passes. This axis simply does not exist in CoxKAN and is the additional interpretive object that SurvKAN provides.
- **Feature edges** show the nonlinear influence of a given covariate on risk.
- **Symbolic expressions after pruning**: once the regularization-induced sparse structure has been pruned, the surviving activation functions can be replaced with symbolic formulas.

The point the authors emphasize is that a clinician can now examine not only how much a variable raises risk, but how that influence itself changes over time.

## Experiments

### Setup

- **Datasets**: 10 public survival datasets
- **Baselines**: AFT, CoxPH, CoxKAN, DeepHit, DeepSurv, RSF (Random Survival Forest), XGBoost
- **Metrics**: C-index, Integrated Brier Score (IBS)
- **Protocol**: 5 repeated hold-out splits with hyperparameter optimization

The C-index measures risk-ranking performance: whether the model assigned higher risk to the patient who in fact experienced the event sooner. The IBS integrates, over the whole time axis, how well the predicted survival curves match the observed data, and therefore measures **calibration** rather than discrimination. Cox-type models tend to be strong at ranking by construction, while producing absolute survival probabilities requires a separately estimated baseline hazard. Where SurvKAN, trained on the full likelihood, lands on IBS is thus the substantive thing to watch in these experiments.

### Results

- **C-index**: best or second-best on 6 of the 10 datasets
- **IBS**: best or second-best on 5 of the 10 datasets
- **Against CoxKAN**: improved on average in both C-index and IBS

The pattern is less one of dominating any single dataset and more one of staying reliably near the top across a diverse set of them. Some baselines are strong on particular datasets, but no baseline is uniformly strong everywhere — the usual picture in survival benchmarks.

### Interpretability case studies

On GBSG2 and METABRIC (both breast cancer cohorts), the hazard patterns learned by SurvKAN are reported to be clinically plausible. The evidence consists of the pruned KAN structure, visualizations of feature-specific hazard contributions, and symbolic formulas, together with the observation that the learned time-varying risk shapes do not conflict with established clinical knowledge.

## Takeaways

- SurvKAN retains CoxKAN's KAN-based interpretability while feeding time in as an input, yielding a fully parametric survival model that learns non-proportional, time-varying hazards. Its central contribution is the **structural removal of the PH assumption**, aimed squarely at the limitation CoxKAN explicitly left open.
- Because it uses the full survival likelihood instead of the partial likelihood, the model produces absolute survival curves and not merely a risk ranking. Reporting both C-index and IBS is consistent with that choice.
- That said, **the novelty relative to CoxKAN is essentially concentrated in two things: the time input and the full likelihood.** The KAN architecture, the regularization terms, the pruning step, and the symbolic conversion pipeline do not depart substantially from CoxKAN's design; this reads less as a methodological innovation than as a natural extension of an existing framework. What justifies the extension is that the assumption it targets (PH) is a genuinely consequential constraint in survival analysis.
- **The interpretability evaluation remains largely qualitative.** No rigorous human study or quantitative interpretability score is provided. The evidence consists of the pruned structure, feature-specific hazard contribution plots, symbolic formulas, and the judgment that the resulting patterns "make clinical sense." The claim that the model is interpretable is stated strongly, but how well it is actually interpreted is not measured.
- **The numerical integration grid creates a cost/accuracy trade-off.** A finer grid gives a more accurate cumulative hazard at greater training cost; a coarser one makes the likelihood itself inaccurate. A sensitivity analysis over grid choice would strengthen the paper.
- **On calibration, the model is sometimes weaker than non-parametric baselines such as RSF.** The relatively lower IBS record (5/10) compared with the C-index (6/10) is not unrelated, and can be read as the cost that parametric assumptions incur when they diverge from the true hazard shape in the data.
- Overall, SurvKAN is the logical next step in the "interpretable KAN-based survival model" lineage: the one that detaches the PH assumption. The open work is the same as for CoxKAN — the stability of the symbolic formulas (does the same expression reappear under a different seed?) and external validation — with the additional question of whether the time patterns captured by the time edges generalize or are artifacts of a particular dataset.
