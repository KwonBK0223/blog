> **Paper**: CoxKAN: Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks for Interpretable, High-Performance Survival Analysis — W. Knottenbelt et al., arXiv:2409.04290, 2024. [arXiv:2409.04290](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.04290)

## TL;DR

CoxKAN is a survival analysis model that predicts the log-partial hazard of the Cox proportional hazards model with a Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN) instead of an MLP. It mitigates KAN's training-speed problem with a fast approximation of the Cox loss, and converts the trained network into a human-readable symbolic hazard formula via L1-norm-based pruning and progressive symbolic fitting. The result aims for the interpretability of CoxPH together with the nonlinear expressiveness of DeepSurv-style models, providing explanations at the level of individual biomarkers.

## Background

Survival analysis, also called time-to-event analysis, models "how long until an event occurs." The event here is an endpoint defined by the researcher — death, cancer recurrence, the onset of a particular complication, and so on.

### Survival function

Letting the event time be a random variable $T$, the survival function is defined as

$$
S(t) = P(T \ge t)
$$

that is, the probability that a patient "survives" beyond time $t$. If the event is death, $S(12) = 0.8$ means an 80% probability of not having died by month 12; if the event is recurrence, it means an 80% probability of not having relapsed by month 12.

### Hazard function

The hazard function $h(t)$ expresses the instantaneous risk that, among those who have survived event-free up to time $t$, the event occurs in the very next infinitesimal interval.

$$
h(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{P(t \le T < t + \Delta t \mid T \ge t)}{\Delta t}
$$

A high hazard means that among the patients who have made it to that point, the risk of the event occurring right afterward is high. The probability density of the event time is the product of the hazard and the survival function:

$$
f(t) = h(t) \, S(t)
$$

The survival function can also be rewritten in terms of the cumulative hazard:

$$
S(t) = \exp\left(-\int_0^t h(u)\, du\right)
$$

The larger the accumulated hazard, the lower the probability of remaining event-free.

### Censoring

Survival data collected from patients consists of three components:

- **Covariates $x$**: predictor variables such as age, sex, disease stage, lab values, treatment information, imaging features
- **Observed time $t$**
- **Event indicator $\delta$**: $\delta = 1$ if the event was observed, $\delta = 0$ for right censoring

Right censoring means the event had not occurred as of the last follow-up, and what happened afterward is unknown. In that case $t$ is the time until the last known contact. Simply discarding censored samples introduces severe bias, so the survival loss must handle censoring explicitly.

### Cox proportional hazards and the log-partial hazard

Cox-family models decompose the total hazard into a time-dependent baseline hazard and a patient-specific risk factor:

$$
h(t \mid x) = h_0(t) \exp\big(\theta(x)\big)
$$

Here $h_0(t)$ is the common baseline risk that varies over time, while $\theta(x)$ is the patient-specific risk determined by the covariates, expressed on a log scale — the **log-partial hazard**. It can be read as a risk score answering "how much more at risk is this patient than another?"

Classical CoxPH assumes $\theta(x) = \beta^\top x$, which is easy to interpret but overly simplistic and introduces significant bias. Deep learning approaches such as DeepSurv and Cox-nnet replace $\theta(x)$ with a neural network and improve performance, but become black boxes. In clinical practice, this lack of interpretability is precisely what blocks real-world adoption.

### Cox loss

The Cox model uses the negative log of the partial likelihood as its loss:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{cox}} = -\sum_{i:\, \delta_i = 1} \left[ \theta(x_i) - \log \sum_{j \in \mathcal{R}(t_i)} \exp\big(\theta(x_j)\big) \right]
$$

where $\mathcal{R}(t_i) = \{ j : t_j \ge t_i \}$ is the risk set at time $t_i$ — the set of patients still event-free at that moment. The appeal of the partial likelihood is that $\theta(x)$ can be learned without ever estimating the baseline hazard $h_0(t)$.

### KAN

KAN is an alternative to the MLP that places learnable activation functions (B-splines) on the edges rather than fixed activations on the nodes. Because the learned activations can be replaced with symbolic expressions, interpretability is high. But applying KAN directly to survival analysis raises two problems: training is slow, which is impractical for high-dimensional datasets, and vanilla symbolic fitting alone cannot handle the complexity of real clinical and genomics data.

## Method

CoxKAN has the KAN directly predict the log-partial hazard $\theta(x)$, and structures training in three stages.

### Stage 1: Training with a fast Cox loss approximation

The computational cost of the Cox loss comes from the risk-set summation $\sum_{j \in \mathcal{R}(t_i)} \exp(\theta(x_j))$. Traversing the risk set anew for every sample costs quadratic time in the batch size. CoxKAN instead sorts samples by observed time and computes the risk-set sums in one pass with a cumulative sum, sharply reducing the computation. This is the key device for mitigating KAN's slow training. When there are many tied observed times, the risk-set computation can be slightly inexact, but the paper reports the effect on actual performance to be small.

The training objective is the Cox loss plus a regularization term:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{cox}} + \lambda R
$$

The regularizer $R$ keeps the model simple and small — preventing overfitting, preparing for pruning, and improving interpretability. It is composed of three terms:

- **L1 norm of the activation functions**: KAN has a learnable activation on every edge. Shrinking the average magnitude of these activations drives unimportant edges close to zero, making subsequent pruning easier.
- **Activation entropy**: rather than every edge being used a little, this encourages only a few important edges to be used strongly. It turns the network from a diffuse structure into a sparse one centered on a handful of key edges.
- **L1 norm of the spline coefficients**: KAN activations are represented as B-splines, and large, complex spline coefficients let the activation curve bend excessively. Keeping the splines simple prevents overfitting.

Hyperparameters are searched efficiently with a Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE).

### Stage 2: Pruning

After training, the mean absolute value — the L1 norm — of each activation function is computed. Activations with small values are judged to contribute almost nothing to prediction, and the corresponding edges are removed; if warranted, nodes and even input features are effectively removed as well. The threshold is chosen based on validation performance. This stage filters out irrelevant noisy features and shrinks the network.

### Stage 3: Progressive symbolic fitting

Each activation function in the pruned network is replaced with a human-readable expression. The core idea is not to hunt for a complex formula from the start, but to try the simplest forms first and escalate complexity only when needed.

1. Train CoxKAN with B-spline activations.
2. Collect input-output pairs for each activation function.
3. Attempt fitting with the simplest linear function first.
4. If the fit is good, substitute the linear form.
5. If not, fit with a predefined set of symbolic operators (e.g. $\exp$, $\log$, $\sin$, $x^2$, $|x|$).
6. If that still fails, resort to PySR symbolic regression.
7. Finally, convert every spline curve into an expression and compose them into a symbolic formula for the entire log-partial hazard.

The final output is a symbolic log-partial hazard formula explaining patient risk, and the authors argue this formula delivers interpretability, generalization, and robustness at once.

## Experiments

The evaluation metric is the **C-index (concordance index)**. Unlike accuracy, which asks whether a prediction is right or wrong, the C-index measures risk-ranking quality: given two patients, did the model assign a higher risk to the one who actually experienced the event sooner? A value of 0.5 is random, 1.0 is perfect ranking.

### Synthetic data

Four synthetic benchmarks designed by the authors. The purpose is not "does it perform well" but rather **can CoxKAN recover a known hazard formula**.

- **Gaussian**: tests recovery of a smooth nonlinear hazard function.
- **Mixed**: tests recovery of a function mixing several nonlinear terms such as trigonometric and polynomial ones.
- **Euclidean**: tests whether distance-based structure can be recovered.
- **Complex**: tests a hard function mixing logs, absolute values, and variable interactions.

CoxKAN successfully recovered the true hazard formula in three of the four. It also successfully pruned away the irrelevant noisy features the authors deliberately injected.

### Clinical data

Evaluated on five clinical datasets: SUPPORT, GBSG, METABRIC, FLCHAIN, and NWTCO. CoxKAN outperformed CoxPH and was competitive with or better than DeepSurv in C-index. More important than raw performance is that it retained its performance after symbolic fitting while surfacing key biomarkers and complex variable interactions — such as age-cancer status interactions — at the level of an explicit formula.

### Genomics data

Also evaluated on the high-dimensional genomics datasets BRCA, STAD, GBM/LGG, LUAD, and KIRC. The question here is whether CoxKAN can handle complex data distributions while still providing meaningful gene-level explanations.

### How is interpretability evaluated?

The paper assesses interpretability along several axes:

- Does it recover the true hazard formula on synthetic data?
- Does it prune the injected irrelevant noisy features?
- Is the derived symbolic formula human-readable?
- Does it rediscover known biomarkers on clinical and genomics datasets?
- Is predictive performance maintained after symbolic fitting?

## Takeaways

- CoxKAN combines the interpretability of Cox survival analysis with the nonlinear expressiveness of deep learning, and through pruning and progressive symbolic fitting it aims to deliver not just performance but biomarker-level explanation.
- The fast approximation of the Cox loss is a substantive contribution that brings KAN's chronic training-speed problem down to a practical level.
- That said, **the proportional hazards assumption is kept intact.** The strong assumption that hazard ratios do not vary over time still underpins the model, and situations where that assumption breaks are not addressed.
- **The interpretability evaluation is partly qualitative.** Criteria like "is the formula human-readable?" and "did it find known biomarkers?" do not reduce to quantitative metrics and depend to some degree on the evaluator's judgment.
- **Overfitting and initialization sensitivity are observed on high-dimensional genomics data.** As the input dimension grows, KAN's parameter count rises quickly, and the symbolic formula that emerges can differ depending on initialization.
- Consequently, the stability of the symbolic formula, control over its complexity, and external validation remain open points. Whether the same formula emerges when the model is retrained on the same data with a different seed will be decisive for real clinical trustworthiness.
