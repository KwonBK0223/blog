> **Paper**: Vertical federated learning for transport mode detection using multi-modality data — Yang et al., *Transportation Research Part C: Emerging Technologies*, Vol. 184, 105546, 2026. [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0968090X26000343)

## TL;DR

- The data useful for Transport Mode Detection (TMD) — IMU, GPS, and mobile phone network data (MPND) — is in practice held by **different parties**: device manufacturers, location service providers, and telecom operators.
- This paper proposes a **vertical federated learning (VFL)** framework for multi-modal TMD that exchanges only intermediate representations, never raw data.
- The key components are (1) **local semi-supervised reconstruction** exploiting unlabeled data, (2) **representation alignment** pulling modality embeddings into a shared latent space, (3) **knowledge distillation** in which the strong modality (IMU) teaches the weak ones, and (4) server-side **attention-based fusion**.
- On the SHL dataset, VFL accuracy reaches 0.930, sharply narrowing the gap to centralized training (0.945); the framework holds up in low-label regimes and supports **single-modality inference without retraining** when modalities are missing.

## Background

### Transport Mode Detection and multi-modality

TMD automatically recognizes and classifies which mode of transport a user is taking (walking, cycling, car, bus, train, etc.) — a subfield of Human Activity Recognition, used for travel recommendation, congestion management, and urban planning.

The modalities are complementary: IMU captures fine-grained motion dynamics, GPS captures macroscopic movement patterns, and MPND (mobile phone network data) provides coarse but broadly available environmental cues. But multi-modal fusion faces practical barriers.

- **Sampling frequency mismatch** across modalities (IMU at 100 Hz vs. low-rate GPS/MPND)
- Differences in spatial resolution and coverage
- The high cost of label collection
- And above all, data is **distributed across different parties**, making integration difficult for privacy and commercial reasons

Here, a party is not a physical device but a data partition unit. The realistic grounding for the vertical setting is that IMU may be managed by the device/OS provider, GPS by the location service provider, and MPND by the telecom operator.

### Why VFL rather than HFL

Horizontal FL aggregates model parameters across clients that share the same feature space, so it is ill-suited to a feature-heterogeneous setting where the modalities themselves differ. In VFL, parties share the same sample IDs but hold different feature subsets; instead of averaging parameters, the server **fuses each local model's intermediate representation** and backpropagates gradients so each local model can be updated. Plain VFL, however, leaves three open problems.

1. **Frequency mismatch**: balancing modalities with different sampling rates
2. **Single-modality inference**: predicting when some modalities are missing
3. **Performance degradation**: letting data-poor parties benefit from strong modalities without dragging down overall performance

## Method

### Problem setup and data processing

Given per-modality sequences $X^{(m)} = [x_1^{(m)}, \dots, x_T^{(m)}]$, $x_t^{(m)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_m}$, trajectories are split whenever the gap between consecutive points exceeds 20 minutes, then divided into fixed-length segments of duration $\Delta t$. Under the assumption that transport modes rarely change within a short window, each segment carries a single mode label.

- **IMU**: 10 channels — 3-axis accelerometer, gyroscope, magnetometer plus barometer — z-score normalized. At 100 Hz it has roughly 100× more samples than the other modalities, so each segment is further split into 20 sub-segments.
- **GPS**: from (latitude, longitude, timestamp) tuples, five features are derived — displacement $d_i$, speed $v_i$, acceleration $a_i$, jerk $j_i$, and heading change $\theta_i$. Jerk is a useful mode discriminator (nearly absent on trains, frequent on buses).
- **MPND**: given its low spatial resolution, only three signal-strength features are used, with drifting errors and the ping-pong effect removed.

Frequency-domain features require uniform sampling and are therefore excluded; all modalities are processed in the time domain.

### Model architecture

**Local (each party)** — one autoencoder per modality. The encoder uses a TCN (dilated, causal convolutions) to extract temporal dependencies efficiently, combines self-attention with a global feature (per-channel maxima replicated along the sequence length and concatenated), and compresses channels with an NIN to produce the intermediate representation $z^{(m)}$. For the high-frequency IMU, sub-segments are stochastically sampled during training, processed independently, and merged by mean pooling — so fast inference is possible even from short IMU sequences. The decoder mitigates information loss in the latent space.

**Server** — each modality's representation is pooled and projected to a common dimension $D$, multi-head **cross-modal attention** learns inter-modality relations, and mean pooling followed by a linear+softmax layer performs classification.

### Training strategy

The total loss is a weighted sum of four terms.

$$\mathcal{L} = \lambda_{rec}\,\mathcal{L}_{rec} + \lambda_{align}\,\mathcal{L}_{align} + \lambda_{cls}\,\mathcal{L}_{cls} + \lambda_{distill}\,\mathcal{L}_{distill}$$

**(1) Local semi-supervised reconstruction** — each party locally minimizes $\mathcal{L}_{rec}^{(m)} = \text{MSE}(x^{(m)}, \hat{x}^{(m)})$. Without touching labels, it learns representations that preserve each modality's intrinsic structure, simultaneously exploiting unlabeled data and regularizing the encoder.

**(2) Representation alignment** — the first term of the alignment loss minimizes pairwise distances between embeddings of different modalities for the same sample ID; the second weakly regularizes ($\beta = 0.1$) each embedding toward the shared centroid $\bar{p} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_m p^{(m)}$. Heterogeneous modalities are thereby aligned in a shared latent space.

**(3) Single-modality inference** — at inference time, missing modalities are handled by duplicating the available embedding into the fusion module.

$$\text{Fusion}(p^{(m)}, -, -) \;\rightarrow\; \text{Fusion}(p^{(m)}, p^{(m)}, p^{(m)})$$

Because alignment leaves the modality embeddings of the same sample pointing in nearly the same direction in latent space, one modality can substitute for the others **without any retraining**.

**(4) Knowledge distillation** — the information gap between modalities is large in the vertical setting. The strongest modality, IMU, serves as teacher; GPS and MPND are students that mimic the teacher's output distribution via KL divergence, while a CE term keeps them learning from ground truth. Whereas CE conveys only the correct answer, KL also conveys the structure of the distribution across classes, correcting the decision boundaries of the weak modalities.

In short, **alignment aligns the feature space, distillation aligns the output space**, and reconstruction stabilizes the representations.

## Experiments

**Data**: the SHL Preview dataset — 2,812 hours of annotated sensor data collected by three participants over seven months in southeast England. Eight transport modes (still, walk, run, bike, car, bus, train, subway).

**Main results (Table 1)**
- The proposed model achieves VFL accuracy $A_{VFL} = 0.930$ vs. 0.945 for the centralized model — a small gap.
- Single-modality inference accuracies are IMU 0.911, GPS 0.706, and MPND 0.282, revealing the disparity in modality strength.
- Ablation: Base 0.875 → +Align 0.900 → +Distill 0.911; Align+Distill 0.926, Align+Att 0.925. By contrast, Distill+Att without alignment is unstable at 0.890. **Alignment is the foundation, with distillation and attention stacked on top.**

**Per-class analysis (Table 2)**: macro F1 0.893. Run/Bike/Car exceed F1 0.95, while Subway/Train lag — GPS weakens underground and the motion patterns of the two modes are similar. It is precisely on these confusable modes that modality fusion pays off most.

**Label-ratio experiments (Table 3)**: accuracy is 0.785 even at a label ratio of $\alpha = 0.1$, 0.930 at $\alpha = 0.5$, and 0.978 at $\alpha = 1.0$. The reconstruction+alignment+distillation combination is what gives the framework its edge in semi-supervised regimes.

**Sensitivity analysis (Tables 4, 5)**: with 10% sample misalignment accuracy drops to 0.842, and to 0.748 at 50%. Attention absorbs mild misalignment, but **exact sample alignment is a structural prerequisite**. Switching the teacher from IMU to GPS drops accuracy to 0.908, and to MPND 0.868 — **the teacher must be a strong modality**. The optimal hyperparameters were a distillation temperature near 0.2 and an alignment weight in the 0.6–0.8 range.

## Takeaways

Rather than chasing SOTA numbers, this work focuses on designing an architecture that works reliably under three real-world constraints: **distributed data, scarce labels, and missing modalities**. Its contribution is a realistic baseline for vertical deep multi-modality learning.

The limitations are worth noting.

1. **Strong sample-alignment assumption** — full alignment of sample IDs is presupposed, and performance collapses as misalignment grows.
2. **Strong-teacher assumption** — KD performance depends on teacher quality, so it may be brittle across domains.
3. **Possible loss of expressiveness** — alignment forces modality embeddings to converge, risking reduced modality diversity.
4. **Fusion limitations** — cross-attention + mean pooling may fall short of modelling higher-order interactions.
5. **No privacy analysis** — inversion and gradient-leakage risks of sharing intermediate representations are not explicitly analyzed.

Even so, the division of labor — alignment (structural alignment), distillation (decision-boundary alignment), reconstruction (representation stabilization) — is crisp, and the design transfers directly to other VFL problems where modalities are held by different parties.
