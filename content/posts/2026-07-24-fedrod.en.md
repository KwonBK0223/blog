> **Paper**: On Bridging Generic and Personalized Federated Learning for Image Classification — Hong-You Chen, Wei-Lun Chao, *ICLR 2022*. [arXiv:2107.00778](https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.00778)

## TL;DR

- Federated learning pursues two distinct goals. **Generic FL (G-FL)** aims to train a single strong global model, while **personalized FL (P-FL)** aims to build a customized model for each client's own test data.
- Under non-IID conditions where clients have different class distributions, these goals conflict. **Empirical risk** helps personalization but makes clients optimize different objectives, hurting the global model; **balanced risk** helps the global model but can degrade personalized performance.
- **FedRoD** (Federated Robust Decoupling) places two predictors on top of a shared feature extractor. The **G-head** is trained with balanced risk and aggregated on the server; the **P-head** is trained with empirical risk and kept local. The personalized prediction is defined as the sum of the two heads' logits.
- The result is a single framework that performs strongly on both G-FL and P-FL at the same time, and a hypernetwork extension further enables zero-shot adaptation to new clients.

## Background

### Generic FL vs personalized FL

Most prior federated learning work has focused on one of two goals.

- **Generic FL**: make the single global model aggregated at the server perform well on generic test data. FedAvg-style methods belong here.
- **Personalized FL**: acknowledge the heterogeneity across clients and give each client $m$ its own model $w_m$ that fits its local test data well.

In general, personalized models from P-FL methods outperform the global model from G-FL on each client's own test data. The problem is that few methods have managed to do well on both goals simultaneously.

### Terminology — global/local vs generic/personalized

The paper carefully separates two pairs of terms that are easy to conflate, and this distinction matters throughout.

- **Global model vs local model** is a distinction of the **training stage**.
  - **Local model**: the model each client obtains right after its local training in a round.
  - **Global model**: the model aggregated from the local models at the server.
- **Generic model vs personalized model** is a distinction of the **test stage**.
  - **Generic model**: the model used for generic future test data.
  - **Personalized model**: the model used for each client's own test data.

In other words, a "local model" is an artifact of training, whereas a "personalized model" is a role at test time. As we will see, one of the paper's key findings is that the local models produced by G-FL algorithms are in fact already strong personalized models.

### Two forms of non-IID

From a classification viewpoint, non-IID data can take two forms.

- **Non-identical class distribution**: the class proportions $P_m(y)$ differ across clients.
- **Non-identical class-conditional distribution**: even for the same class, the feature distribution $P_m(x \mid y)$ differs across clients.

The latter is hard to mitigate without direct access to client data. The former, however — differences in class distribution — can largely be mitigated by **imposing a consistent training objective across clients**, which is the central argument of the paper. Concretely, a class-balanced objective makes local training consistent across clients, and this in turn makes the global model robust to non-IID conditions.

### The tension between empirical risk and balanced risk

**Empirical risk** is the average loss over the data a client actually holds. Since it follows client $m$'s data distribution directly, minimizing it is **good for the personalized model** that must fit that client's test data. But when clients have different class distributions, each client ends up optimizing a different objective, which can be **bad for the global model** obtained by averaging.

**Balanced risk** is a loss that corrects for class imbalance. Plain empirical risk is pulled toward the classes a client holds in abundance — if a client has many class-0 examples, the model gravitates toward class 0. Balanced risk, implemented via re-weighting or a balanced softmax loss, forces the model to treat classes evenly, so that even with different class proportions, every client shares the **consistent objective** of classifying all classes well. This benefits the global model, but it can **hurt personalized performance**, because the local models no longer optimize the client's empirical risk.

In short, empirical risk favors P-FL and balanced risk favors G-FL, and each undermines the other's goal. FedRoD resolves this tension by assigning the two losses to two different predictors.

## Method (FedRoD)

### Architecture — two losses, two predictors

FedRoD decomposes the model into a shared feature extractor and two heads.

$$z = f(x; \theta)$$

- $f(x;\theta)$: the **feature extractor** shared by all clients.

$$\hat{y}_G = h^G(z; \psi)$$

- **G-head (generic predictor)**: the head for the global model, trained with the **balanced risk**.

$$\hat{y}_P = h^G(z; \psi) + h^P(z; \phi_m)$$

- **P-head (personalized predictor)**: a head $h^P(z;\phi_m)$ dedicated to client $m$, trained with the **empirical risk**. Crucially, it is not an independent predictor but a **logit-level add-on** on top of the G-head. The personalized prediction is thus composed of a shared prediction plus a client-specific correction, and the paper indeed defines it as the sum of the G-head and P-head logits.

With this design, the feature extractor and G-head are trained under a balanced objective that is consistent across clients — safe to aggregate — while the P-head is free to fit each client's empirical distribution, only needing to learn a small correction on top of what the G-head already predicts.

### Training workflow

The overall pipeline follows FedAvg and proceeds as follows.

1. The server sends the shared feature extractor $\theta$ and the G-head $\psi$ to the clients.
2. Each client trains both losses simultaneously on its local data.
3. $\theta, \psi$ are trained with the **balanced risk**.
4. The personalized head $\phi_m$ is trained with the **empirical risk**.
5. Each client sends only $\theta_m, \psi_m$ back to the server.
6. The server averages only $\theta, \psi$, as in FedAvg.
7. $\phi_m$ stays local at each client.
8. At test time, generic data is handled with $\theta, \psi$.
9. Client-specific data is handled with $\theta, \psi, \phi_m$.

Only the generic part ($\theta, \psi$) is communicated and aggregated; the personalized part ($\phi_m$) remains on each client. Since the P-head is a lightweight add-on, the extra cost is small.

### Hypernetwork extension — FedRoD-hyper

Instead of storing a separate $\phi_m$ per client, FedRoD-hyper **generates** the personalized head from the client's class distribution $a_m$.

$$\phi_m = H^P(a_m; \nu)$$

$H^P$ is a small shared hypernetwork that, given a client's class distribution, outputs a personalized predictor tailored to that client; its parameters $\nu$ are learned jointly by the clients. The advantage is that a personalized head can be produced immediately for a new client as long as its class distribution is known. The paper exploits this in two ways.

- **Zero-shot adaptation**: a personalized model is provided for a new client using only its class distribution, without any of its data.
- **Fine-tuning initialization**: it offers a better starting point when a new client fine-tunes on its own data.

## Experiments

The experimental results support three main claims.

- **Strong on both G-FL and P-FL**: unlike prior methods specialized for one side, FedRoD achieves strong generic and personalized performance simultaneously — the G-head alone yields a robust global model, while the G-head plus P-head yields a well-fitted personalized model per client.
- **Local models of G-FL are already strong personalized models**: an intriguing observation is that the local models emerging from the local training phase of generic FL algorithms such as FedAvg are already surprisingly strong personalized models. Starting local training from the weight-averaged global model each round acts as an **implicit regularization** — the weight-average initialization itself is a strong regularizer.
- **Class-balanced objectives help generic FL**: when clients have different class distributions, making local training consistent via a class-balanced objective improves generic FL performance.

In addition, FedRoD-hyper provides zero-shot adaptation and an effective fine-tuning initialization for new clients that never participated in training.

## Takeaways

FedRoD turns a simple principle — generic and personalized are different goals, so they deserve different losses and different predictors — into an architecture. To summarize:

- **Explicit separation of roles**: what a global model should do (treat all classes evenly) and what a personalized model should do (fit my own distribution) are different, and collapsing them into one model with one loss was the limitation of prior work. FedRoD separates the roles into a balanced-risk G-head and an empirical-risk P-head, while the logit add-on design lets the two roles share representations.
- **Choosing what to aggregate**: just as FedBN handled feature shift by deciding "do not average the BN layers," FedRoD handles class distribution shift by deciding "do not average the P-head." It reaffirms that choosing what to share and what to keep local is a central design axis of personalized FL.
- **Reinterpreting local models**: the observation that G-FL local models are already good personalized models thanks to implicit regularization serves as a bridge between the G-FL and P-FL literatures, and has become a frequently cited insight.
- **Distribution-conditioned personalization**: the hypernetwork extension, which generates a personalized head from a class distribution, points to a practical direction — adapting to new clients from distribution information alone, without their data.

When differing class distributions are the dominant source of non-IID, FedRoD stands as a concise baseline that gives up neither G-FL nor P-FL.
