> **Paper**: Opportunities and Challenges in Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI): A Survey — Arun Das, Paul Rad, arXiv preprint, 2020. [arXiv:2006.11371](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.11371)

## TL;DR

As deep learning models enter mission-critical domains like healthcare and autonomous driving, the ability to answer "why did the model make this prediction" has become as important as performance itself. This paper is a survey that systematizes explainable AI (XAI) techniques along three classification axes: **Scope (Local vs Global)**, **Methodology (Backpropagation vs Perturbation)**, and **Usage (Intrinsic vs Post-hoc)**. It places representative techniques — from Activation Maximization to LRP, Grad-CAM, Integrated Gradients, RISE, and TCAV — within this framework, and also addresses how to evaluate the quality of explanations and the fundamental limitations XAI currently faces.

## Background

Machine learning models — deep learning models in particular — are inherently black boxes. Data goes in and predictions come out, but the intermediate process is hard for humans to understand. This has given rise to the following demands.

- **Transparency**: decisions should be justifiable with grounds a human can understand.
- **Trust**: confidence in the model's decisions should be strengthened.
- **Fairness**: by inspecting the model's behavior as a whole, biases should be discoverable and correctable.

The paper defines XAI as "a set of tools, techniques, and algorithms through which the AI itself derives explanations, enabling humans to understand and correctly interpret the AI's behavior and final outputs." Typical use cases include explaining "why turn left" in autonomous driving or "why was this diagnosed as cancer" in medical data; conversely, one can also extract insights from the model's explanations that humans had not discovered. The perspective is that by adding interpretability and a human-inspection loop to the standard ML pipeline, a model previously evaluated only by generalization error can be improved by incorporating human experience as well.

## Method: Three Classification Axes

### Axis 1 — Scope: What Is Being Explained

- **Local**: generates an explanation for a single data instance. There is one explanation map $g$ for each $x \in X$.
- **Global**: explains the model as a whole unit — a single explanation map for the entire data group $X$.

**Representative Local techniques**

- **Activation Maximization**: finds the input pattern that maximizes the activation of a particular hidden unit, revealing what each layer has learned.

$$x^* = \arg\max_{x \,\text{s.t.}\, \|x\| = \rho} z_{ij}(\theta, x)$$

- **Saliency Map**: computes the gradient of the output class per pixel to derive pixel importance, assuming that pixels with larger gradients have greater influence on the result.
- **LRP (Layer-wise Relevance Propagation)**: decomposes the output prediction via backpropagation to obtain relevance scores for the input features. The propagation rule for a simple neural network is as follows.

$$R_{i \leftarrow j} = R(z_j)\frac{x_i w_{ij}}{y_j + \varepsilon\, \mathrm{sign}(y_j)}, \qquad R(x) = \sum_j R_{i \leftarrow j}$$

**Representative Global techniques**

- **Class Model Visualization**: extends Activation Maximization globally, generating class-representative images that maximize the scoring function $S_c(I)$ used in training.
- **CAV/TCAV (Concept Activation Vectors)**: learns a direction vector that separates a human-understandable concept (positive concept $P_c$) from other concepts ($N$), and quantifies via directional derivatives how sensitive a class prediction is to that concept.
- **SpRAy (Spectral Relevance Analysis)**: performs spectral clustering of inputs based on relevance scores obtained from LRP, summarizing the model's decision patterns at the population level.

Another major thread in the global family is reducing a complex deep model to an interpretable surrogate — a linear model or decision tree — to show "how" it behaves.

### Axis 2 — Methodology: How It Is Computed

- **Backpropagation (gradient) based**: uses backpropagated gradients to determine neuron influence or input/output relevance.
  - **Saliency Maps**: the longest-used method for interpreting CNN predictions, showing which parts of an image are relevant to the classification.
  - **Grad-CAM**: combines the per-channel activations of the last convolutional layer with Global Average Pooling-based weights to build a class activation map, visualizing class-specific evidence regions while preserving spatial information.
  - **Integrated Gradients (IG)**: computes attributions by integrating gradients along the path from a baseline $x'$ to the input $x$.

$$IG_j(x, x') = (x_j - x'_j) \int_{\alpha=0}^{1} \frac{\partial F(x' + \alpha(x - x'))}{\partial x_j}\, \partial\alpha$$

  - **Expected Gradients (EG)**: replaces IG's integral with an expectation over the data distribution, extending the method so domain knowledge can be encoded into the training stage.
- **Perturbation based**: determines relevance from how the output changes when input features are varied.
  - **DeConvNet**: runs in the reverse direction of a CNN, mapping feature maps back into input pixel space to show what each layer learns.
  - **RISE**: perturbs the input image by multiplying it with random masks, obtains the black-box model's confidence scores, and builds a saliency map as the weighted sum of the masks that produced high outputs.

### Axis 3 — Usage: Where It Applies

- **Intrinsic**: explainability is built into the network architecture itself. Being specialized to that architecture, it is hard to transfer to other architectures.
- **Post-hoc**: applicable after the fact to an already trained network, regardless of model architecture. This is the more practically useful side.

## Evaluation: How to Assess Explanations

Quantifying the quality of explanations themselves is another challenge in XAI, and the paper introduces the main evaluation frameworks.

- **System Causability Scale (SCS)**: scores ten elements important from the human-AI interface perspective on a Likert scale (1–5) and aggregates them as $SCS = \sum_i Rating_i / 50$.
- **BAM (Benchmarking Attribution Methods)**: measures how accurate feature attributions and their relative importances are, by constructing functionally equivalent conditions across models and inputs.
- **Faithfulness & Monotonicity**: Faithfulness computes the correlation between feature importance scores and the actual effect of those features on performance; Monotonicity measures contribution by incrementally adding features in order of importance.
- **Human-grounded Evaluation**: benchmarks explanation models against evaluations from a variety of people, collecting opinions broadly to remove human bias as much as possible.

## Limitations & Takeaways

The current limitations of XAI the paper points out are clear.

1. **Explanation itself remains hard**: humans often struggle to reason correctly even when looking at the explanations XAI produces.
2. **Quantitative evaluation is very difficult**: cases have been reported (common to Simple Gradient, DeepLIFT, and IG) where a slight perturbation of the input leaves the predicted class unchanged yet dramatically alters the feature-importance map, leaving fundamental doubts about the stability and reliability of explanations.

Even so, the Scope × Methodology × Usage taxonomy this survey provides is a useful map for quickly locating the position and characteristics of any new explanation technique. In particular, it lays out at a glance why post-hoc, gradient-based techniques (Grad-CAM and the IG family) became the practical standard, and what gaps perturbation-based and concept-based techniques fill as alternatives — making this a solid entry point into XAI.
