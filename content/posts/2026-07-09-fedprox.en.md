> **Paper**: Federated Optimization in Heterogeneous Networks — Tian Li, Anit Kumar Sahu, Manzil Zaheer, Maziar Sanjabi, Ameet Talwalkar, Virginia Smith, *MLSys 2020*. [arXiv:1812.06127](https://arxiv.org/abs/1812.06127)

## TL;DR

FedProx is an optimization framework that adds a single proximal term, anchored at the global model, to the local objective of FedAvg. The term keeps local updates from drifting too far from the current global model, mitigating the local drift induced by non-IID data, and at the same time makes it safe to aggregate updates from clients that performed different amounts of local work. To support this theoretically, the authors introduce the notion of a $\gamma$-inexact solution — local problems need not be solved exactly — together with a bounded dissimilarity assumption that quantifies the degree of heterogeneity, and derive convergence guarantees on top of them. Empirically, FedProx improves absolute test accuracy by roughly 22 percentage points on average over FedAvg in highly heterogeneous settings.

## Background

### Two kinds of heterogeneity in federated learning

The motivation for federated learning is straightforward: useful training data is scattered across a large number of edge devices, and privacy concerns and communication costs make it impractical to collect that data centrally. FedAvg is what made this setting practical, and it remains the standard baseline against which most subsequent FL algorithms are measured.

Real federated deployments, however, exhibit two forms of heterogeneity that have no counterpart in data-center distributed training.

- **Statistical heterogeneity**: each client's data is generated by that user's own usage patterns, so it does not represent the global distribution. The data is non-IID, and the minimizer of each local objective $F_k$ points in a different direction.
- **Systems heterogeneity**: participating devices differ in hardware, network conditions, and battery status. It is therefore impossible for all of them to perform the same amount of computation within a fixed time budget.

### Limitations of FedAvg

FedAvg assumes that every selected client performs a fixed number of local epochs $E$ in each round. That assumption collides head-on with both forms of heterogeneity.

First, from a systems perspective, FedAvg does not allow devices to perform variable amounts of local work in response to their underlying constraints. Slow devices — **stragglers** — that cannot complete $E$ epochs within the given time window are typically simply dropped from the round. In effect, only the fastest devices contribute to training, which biases participation itself.

Second, from a statistical perspective, FedAvg has been observed to empirically diverge when data is not identically distributed across devices. The number of local epochs is a critical hyperparameter: under non-IID data, the more local epochs a client runs, the further its model is pulled toward its own local optimum, and the averaged global model can actually get worse. Reducing the number of local epochs, on the other hand, undercuts the communication efficiency that motivated FedAvg in the first place.

Third, **convergence guarantees are lacking** for exactly the regime that causes these empirical problems. No prior analysis simultaneously accounted for non-IID data and partial device participation.

Earlier work did attempt to address heterogeneity, but much of it relied on unrealistic assumptions — for instance, that all devices participate in every round, or that the data distribution satisfies restrictive conditions. FedProx starts from a different premise: keep the structure of FedAvg intact and address both forms of heterogeneity with a minimal modification.

## Method

### The proximal term

FedProx defines the local problem solved by client $k$ at round $t$ as follows.

$$
\min_{w} \; h_k(w; w^t) = F_k(w) + \frac{\mu}{2}\lVert w - w^t \rVert^2
$$

- $F_k(w)$: the local empirical loss of client $k$
- $\frac{\mu}{2}\lVert w - w^t \rVert^2$: a proximal term penalizing deviation from the current global model $w^t$
- With $\mu = 0$, FedProx reduces exactly to FedAvg.

It is worth stressing that this term is not ordinary L2 regularization. Standard L2 weight decay pushes the weights themselves toward zero; the proximal term instead **anchors the local model to the current global model $w^t$**. The reference point is the global model, not the origin.

The paper's central claim is that this one term delivers two benefits at once.

1. **Handling statistical heterogeneity**: by constraining local updates to stay close to the initial global model, it removes the need to carefully hand-tune the number of local epochs to prevent drift. Even with a large amount of local work, updates do not blow up.
2. **Handling systems heterogeneity**: it makes it safe to incorporate variable amounts of local work from different devices into the aggregate.

### $\gamma$-inexact solutions

To formalize the second benefit, the authors explicitly introduce a condition stating that local problems need not be solved exactly. A point $w_k^{t+1}$ returned by client $k$ at round $t$ is called a $\gamma_k^t$-inexact solution if

$$
\lVert \nabla h_k(w_k^{t+1}; w^t) \rVert \le \gamma_k^t \, \lVert \nabla h_k(w^t; w^t) \rVert, \qquad \gamma_k^t \in [0, 1]
$$

The interpretation is intuitive. The right-hand side is the gradient magnitude before local optimization begins (that is, at $w^t$), and the left-hand side is the gradient magnitude remaining after local training. Their ratio is $\gamma_k^t$, so:

- $\gamma_k^t = 0$: the local objective was optimized essentially exactly
- small $\gamma_k^t$: a substantial amount of local computation was performed
- large $\gamma_k^t$: only a little training was done — a **partial update**

$\gamma$ is thus a theoretical expression of how faithfully each client carried out its local update. In practice it corresponds to the number of epochs run, the number of local steps taken, or the amount of update work completed within the available time.

### Accepting partial updates

Because of this definition, FedProx does not discard stragglers. Where FedAvg drops any device that fails to complete $E$ epochs, FedProx allows clients to have different values of $\gamma_k^t$ in each round and folds even under-trained updates into the aggregate. A resource-constrained device sends a partial update with a large $\gamma$; a well-provisioned device sends a more refined update with a small $\gamma$. Neither is excluded, so participation bias is reduced.

The algorithmic structure remains almost identical to FedAvg. The server-side aggregation and the communication protocol are unchanged; only one term is added to the local objective. This is the basis for the paper describing itself as a lightweight framework.

## Convergence & Experiments

### Quantifying heterogeneity: bounded dissimilarity

If the degree of non-IID-ness is unbounded, no algorithm can guarantee convergence, so a measure of heterogeneity is needed. The authors define it in terms of how far local gradients deviate from the global gradient. For a randomly sampled client, if

$$
\mathbb{E}_k \left[ \lVert \nabla F_k(w) \rVert^2 \right] \le \lVert \nabla f(w) \rVert^2 B^2
$$

holds, the devices are said to be **$B$-locally dissimilar** at that point. $B = 1$ means every local gradient equals the global gradient, corresponding to the IID case, and larger $B$ means greater heterogeneity. In short, $B$ compresses the degree of statistical heterogeneity into a single number.

The conclusion of the convergence analysis is that $\mu$ plays the role of counteracting this heterogeneity. The greater the heterogeneity (the larger $B$), the larger the $\mu$ required; with a sufficiently large $\mu$, one can show that the global objective decreases in each round. However, a larger $\mu$ also suppresses local progress and slows convergence, which makes $\mu$ a **dial trading off stability against learning speed**. In practice, an adaptive heuristic that increases $\mu$ when the loss rises and decreases it when the loss falls steadily works well.

### Experimental setup

Evaluation is carried out on five datasets: Synthetic, MNIST, FEMNIST, Shakespeare, and Sent140. Backbones are chosen to match the task — multinomial logistic regression for Synthetic, MNIST, and FEMNIST, and a 2-layer LSTM for next-character prediction on Shakespeare and sentiment classification on Sent140. Since the contribution is the optimization framework rather than the model, standard architectures are used as-is.

Systems heterogeneity is simulated by varying the fraction of stragglers across 0%, 50%, and 90%. In each round, that fraction of clients fails to complete the prescribed amount of local work; FedAvg drops them, whereas FedProx accepts them as partial updates.

### Results

- **Stability**: with the proximal term, the variance of the training curves is visibly reduced. The divergent behavior FedAvg exhibits under non-IID data is mitigated, and training does not collapse even when the number of local epochs is increased.
- **Accuracy**: in highly heterogeneous settings, FedProx reports an average improvement of roughly 22 percentage points in absolute test accuracy over FedAvg.
- **Value of including stragglers**: the higher the straggler fraction, the larger FedProx's gain. At the 90% setting, FedAvg ends up training on only a handful of fast devices and degrades substantially, while FedProx still exploits information from partially trained devices.
- **Effect of $\mu$**: settings that are unstable at $\mu = 0$ (i.e., FedAvg) converge stably at an appropriate $\mu$, and the optimal $\mu$ grows with the heterogeneity of the data.

## Takeaways

- FedProx is a lightweight modification of FedAvg — a single proximal term $\frac{\mu}{2}\lVert w - w^t \rVert^2$ — yet it is economically designed in that it targets statistical and systems heterogeneity simultaneously.
- The $\gamma$-inexact solution removes the unrealistic assumption that all clients perform the same amount of local work. As a result, stragglers can be included through partial updates instead of being dropped, which reduces participation bias.
- A single scalar $\mu$ balances stability against convergence speed. Greater heterogeneity calls for a larger $\mu$, but too large a value effectively neutralizes local training, so the choice is data-dependent and requires tuning.
- Defining the magnitude of heterogeneity as a single quantity, $B$-local dissimilarity, gave the field a framing that later theoretical work on FL repeatedly reuses.
- That said, the proximal term only restrains drift; it does not correct the directional discrepancy between client gradients itself. That gap is precisely what later work such as SCAFFOLD, which introduces control variates, sets out to address.
