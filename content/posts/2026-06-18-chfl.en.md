> **Paper**: CHFL: A Collaborative Hierarchical Federated Intrusion Detection System for Vehicular Networks — P. H. Mirzaee, M. Shojafar, H. Cruickshank, R. Tafazolli, in *Proc. 27th IEEE Symposium on Computers and Communications (ISCC)*, 2022. [IEEE Xplore](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9913013)

## TL;DR

A collaborative HFL-based intrusion detection system that dispenses with fixed edge servers: vehicles form proximity groups and **elect a leading vehicle through an in-group election**, which then serves as the edge server. The training itself is FedAvg extended to a three-tier structure (end vehicle – leading vehicle – server).

## Background

The starting point is the chronic problems of applying FL in vehicular networks: **high latency and limited computation/communication resources**. Instead of having every vehicle communicate directly with an edge server, the idea is that letting a group leader act as an intermediate tier makes the communication structure more scalable.

The authors claim the following contributions.

- A more scalable and reliable collaborative hierarchical federated intrusion detection system for vehicular networks
- A **formalized rank-score computation** to optimally identify the leading vehicle that bridges end vehicles and the server
- An increased number of participants sharing the learning model, via a cooperative communication scheme between end vehicles and the leader in direct communication mode
- Lower prediction loss than conventional FL

## Method

### Collaborative Hierarchical Federated Architecture

Individual vehicles do not communicate directly with an edge server. The workflow is as follows.

1. Within a proximity group, each vehicle computes its own rank according to defined parameters and shares it with one-hop neighbors. The highest-ranked vehicle becomes the **leading vehicle**, taking on second-tier responsibilities: communicating with the server and receiving the global model.
2. Once the server initializes the leading vehicle with the global model, the leader distributes it to all vehicles in the group.
3. The leader trains its own model, aggregates the local models of its neighbors, and averages all models to produce the second-tier model update.
4. After local aggregation finishes at the leaders, the server aggregates all group models to perform global averaging.
5. Before the next round begins, the leader monitors its own rank score to verify it is still qualified to remain the leader.

### Rank Computation

The rank score takes roughly the following form.

$$\text{Rank} = \text{Residual Energy} + \text{Data Rate} - \text{Distance} - \text{Speed} - \text{Path Loss}$$

- **Residual energy**: the remaining energy available to serve as leader. Since the leader handles local aggregation and server communication, more energy is better.
- **Data rate**: the rate at which the leader can communicate with the server or nearby vehicles.
- **Distance**: the average distance to nearby vehicles. Being closer reduces in-group communication delay and loss, so larger distances are penalized.
- **Speed**: the vehicle's relative speed/mobility. Fast-moving vehicles reduce group stability and are thus unsuitable as leaders.
- **Path loss**: the signal loss between the vehicle and the network station. It grows with distance or poor channel conditions and counts against the score.

The leader monitors its own rank after every global communication, and if the current rank drops below a fixed fraction (0.8) of its rank at election time, it requests a re-election. The leader can therefore change from round to round.

### Training Process

Training follows the FedAvg structure.

- **End vehicle**: trains a DNN on local data
- **Leading vehicle**: computes a weighted average of nearby end vehicles' models
- **Server**: computes a weighted average of the leading vehicles' group models

The backbone model is a 5-layer DNN.

## Experiments

- **Dataset**: CICIDS 2017
- Reports lower prediction loss compared to conventional flat FL.

## Takeaways

- What distinguishes this paper is that it **uses the vehicles themselves as a dynamic edge tier** instead of relying on infrastructure (fixed edge servers).
- The learning algorithm is a standard extension of FedAvg to HFL; the core contribution is the rank formulation for leader election together with its maintenance and re-election mechanism.
- Since a leader's eligibility is determined by physical-layer properties — energy, communication quality, mobility — this is a good example of encoding the realistic constraints of vehicular environments into the training architecture.
