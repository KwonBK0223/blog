> **Paper**: LB-SimTSC: An Efficient Similarity-Aware Graph Neural Network for Semi-Supervised Time Series Classification — Wenjie Xi, Arnav Jain, Li Zhang, Jessica Lin, arXiv preprint, 2023. [arXiv:2301.04838](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.04838)

## TL;DR

For time series classification with extremely few labels, SimTSC — which combines a GCN with DTW — was powerful, but it required computing $O(L^2)$ DTW distances for every pair of instances, making it practically unusable on large datasets. LB-SimTSC replaces DTW with its lower bound, **LB_Keogh**. Since LB_Keogh is computed in $O(L)$ yet preserves the relative proximity relationships between instances, graph construction becomes 71x faster on average and up to 104x faster, while classification accuracy remains statistically comparable to SimTSC.

## Background

### Time Series Classification and the Semi-Supervised Setting

Time series classification (TSC) is the problem of learning patterns in time series data to determine which category a new time series belongs to. Approaches fall broadly into **distance-based** methods (DTW being the classic example), which compute distances between instances under a given metric and classify nearby instances together, and **feature-based** methods, which extract intrinsic features of the series for classification. In real-world settings, labeled time series are often scarce, so semi-supervised learning — using a small amount of labeled data together with a large amount of unlabeled data — becomes important.

### DTW and SimTSC

**DTW (Dynamic Time Warping)** is an algorithm that measures the similarity between two time series of differing speed or length via nonlinear alignment. Because it absorbs differences in length and speed, it serves as the standard distance in time series analysis, but it requires $O(L^2)$ computation for series of length $L$.

**SimTSC** is a semi-supervised method combining a GCN with DTW to classify few-labeled time series. It builds a DTW pairwise similarity matrix over the entire dataset, converts it into a graph, and propagates the features extracted by a backbone (ResNet) through the GCN, allowing label information to flow to unlabeled instances. It is robust and accurate, but the quadratic complexity of the graph construction step makes it hard to scale to large datasets.

### Lower Bounds and LB_Keogh

A set $E$ is bounded below if $\exists\, m \in \mathbb{R}\ \text{s.t.}\ a \ge m\ \forall a \in E$, in which case $m$ is called a lower bound of $E$. LB_Keogh is a lower bound of the DTW distance, providing a value less than or equal to the true DTW value at a much cheaper cost. It is a classic technique originally devised for pruning — quickly filtering candidates in DTW-based nearest neighbor search.

## Method

The problem setting is transductive semi-supervised classification. The training data is $X^{train} = X^{labeled} + X^{unlabeled}$, with $X^{labeled} = [X_1, \dots, X_{n'}]$, $y^{labeled} = [y_1, \dots, y_{n'}],\ y_i \in \{1, \dots, C\}$, and the unlabeled test data is visible at training time.

### Step 1: Batch Sampling

A batch $X^B$ of size $m$ is constructed, half drawn from $(X^{labeled}, y^{labeled})$ and the other half from $X^{unlabeled}$. To avoid training imbalance, each class is guaranteed an equal number of samples.

### Step 2: Computing the LB_Keogh Pairwise Matrix

For two time series $X_i, X_j$ in the batch, LB_Keogh builds upper and lower envelopes around $X_i$ with warping allowance $r$.

$$u_{i,k} = \max\left([X_{i,k-r}, \dots, X_{i,k+r}]\right), \qquad l_{i,k} = \min\left([X_{i,k-r}, \dots, X_{i,k+r}]\right)$$

The distance is then defined by summing only the extent to which $X_j$ falls outside the envelope.

$$d^{LB}(X_i, X_j) = \sqrt{\sum_{k=1}^{L} \begin{cases} (X_{j,k} - u_{i,k})^2, & \text{if } X_{j,k} > u_{i,k} \\ (X_{j,k} - l_{i,k})^2, & \text{if } X_{j,k} < l_{i,k} \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}}$$

The pairwise matrix over the batch is defined as $D_{LB}(i,j) = d^{LB}(X_i, X_j)$. Since the operations are independent per time step, it is $O(L)$ and easy to parallelize, making it well suited to GPU implementation.

### Step 3: Graph Construction

Distances are converted to similarities ($\alpha$ is a scaling parameter),

$$A_{i,j} = \frac{1}{\exp(\alpha D_{i,j})}$$

and only $K$ neighbors are kept per row to form a sparse adjacency matrix. If the candidate set with zero LB_Keogh distance, $\mathcal{Q}_i = \{k \mid D_{i,k} = 0\}$, contains at least $K$ elements, $K$ are chosen from it at random; otherwise, the shortfall is filled with the instances of smallest distance.

$$\bar{A}_{i,j} = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{K}, & j \sim \mathcal{Q}_i \text{ and } |\mathcal{Q}_i| \ge K \\ A_{i,j}, & A_{i,j} \in \mathrm{argTopK}(A_i) \text{ and } |\mathcal{Q}_i| < K \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

The edge weights of the final graph $G^B(V, E)$ are obtained by row normalization: $E_{i,j} = \bar{A}_{i,j} / \sum_{b=1}^{m} \bar{A}_{i,b}$.

### Step 4: Graph Convolution and Classification

Using the same ResNet backbone $f$ as SimTSC, feature embeddings $z = f(X^B)$ are computed, then information is propagated over the graph with a GCN and classified with a softmax.

$$z^{l} = GCN(G^B, z^{l-1}), \qquad o = \mathrm{Softmax}(z^{l})$$

## Experiments

Evaluation covers the ten largest datasets in the UCR archive, varying the number of labels per class from 5 to 30.

- **vs 1NN-DTW**: with 5 labels the score is tied 5:5, but with 10 or more labels LB-SimTSC wins on every dataset except UWGLAll (9 wins, 1 loss at 10 labels, p-value 0.042).
- **vs SimTSC**: graph construction is 71x faster on average and up to 104x faster (Mallat dataset). The total graph construction time across all ten datasets is 88.7 hours for SimTSC vs 1.2 hours for LB-SimTSC. Yet classification accuracy shows no statistically significant difference under any label setting (p-values 0.232–0.695).

What is interesting is that performance holds even though LB_Keogh provides "less accurate" warping information than DTW. The paper's interpretation: the aggregation the GCN uses to propagate label information does not require exact distance weights, and even the zero entries in the LB_Keogh matrix indicate that the corresponding instances fall within the envelope region — meaning they already share a degree of warping similarity.

## Takeaways

- As the first work to connect a lower-bound-distance-based graph to a GCN for semi-supervised time series classification, it offers an efficient alternative to DTW-based approaches.
- By reducing time complexity from $O(L^2)$ to $O(L)$ while retaining the essential properties of time warping, it effectively solves the scalability problem for large datasets.
- The design philosophy of approximating upstream computation based on "the precision of information the downstream module (the GCN) actually needs" is a lesson worth generalizing to other graph-based pipelines. More often than one might expect, preserving only the relative proximity structure — not exact distances — is enough.
