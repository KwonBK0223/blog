> **Paper**: FedBN: Federated Learning on Non-IID Features via Local Batch Normalization — Xiaoxiao Li, Meirui Jiang, Xiaofei Zhang, Michael Kamp, Qi Dou, *ICLR 2021*. [arXiv:2102.07623](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.07623)

## TL;DR

- Prior non-IID work in federated learning has mostly focused on **label distribution skew**, but in practice **feature shift** — where the input feature distribution differs across clients — is common.
- In this setting, FedAvg averages every layer and smears out each client's feature statistics, hurting performance.
- **FedBN** trains just like FedAvg but **keeps the Batch Normalization (BN) layers local to each client instead of averaging them**. It introduces no extra hyperparameters and drops directly into the existing FedAvg pipeline.
- Across a simplified theoretical analysis, a digit benchmark, and real-world multi-domain datasets, FedBN outperforms FedAvg and FedProx under feature-shift non-IID conditions.

## Background

### Two kinds of non-IID

Non-IID in federated learning is not a single phenomenon but splits into distinct types. Prior FL work has largely addressed **label distribution skew**, where clients hold different proportions of classes. This paper instead focuses on **feature shift**, where the input feature distribution $P_i(x)$ itself differs across clients.

Feature shift arises very naturally in the real world.

- **Medical imaging**: different hospitals use different MRI/CT scanners, producing images with different intensity and contrast.
- **Autonomous driving**: vehicles differ in sensor characteristics, driving environments, and road conditions.
- **Digit recognition**: even for the same digit classification task, image styles differ across MNIST, SVHN, and USPS.

### Covariate shift and concept shift

Feature shift can be refined into two related notions.

- **Covariate shift**: the distribution of the input $x$ differs across clients, but the way the label $y$ is determined given $x$, namely $P(y \mid x)$, is the same. For example, image contrast varies across hospitals, yet the criterion for judging whether a tumor is present is identical.
- **Concept shift**: the label proportions are similar, but the appearance of features within the same class differs across clients. Even if every hospital has a similar healthy/diseased ratio, the appearance of diseased scans may look different depending on the equipment.

The central claim of FedBN is that when feature distributions differ across clients like this, averaging the BN statistics and BN parameters can actually degrade performance.

## Method

### Core idea — keep BN local

FedBN trains just like FedAvg but **keeps only the Batch Normalization layers local to each client instead of averaging them**. Conv/FC layers are aggregated at the server in the FedAvg manner, while the BN layers stay local at each client. It adds no extra hyperparameters and applies easily to existing FL networks that contain BN layers.

The workflow is as follows.

1. The server broadcasts the global **non-BN parameters** to the clients.
2. Each client trains the full model on its local data with its **local BN**.
3. After training, each client uploads its model parameters to the server.
4. The server averages **only the non-BN layers**.
5. The **BN layers remain as client-specific local parameters**.
6. The averaged non-BN layers are broadcast again, and each client continues training while correcting for feature shift through its own BN.

### Why local BN works

The Batch Normalization transform can be written as

$$y = \gamma \frac{x - \mu_B}{\sqrt{\sigma_B^2 + \epsilon}} + \beta$$

where $\mu_B$ and $\sigma_B^2$ are **client-specific feature statistics** estimated from the batch, and $\gamma$ and $\beta$ are **learnable scale/shift parameters** that re-adjust the normalized features. In short, $\gamma$ rescales the magnitude of a feature after normalization.

When feature distributions differ across clients, not only the normalizing statistics $\mu_B, \sigma_B^2$ but also the scale/shift parameters $\gamma, \beta$ should differ per client. Keeping them client-specific rather than averaging them lets the **local BN absorb each client's feature shift**, while the remaining non-BN layers learn a representation shared across clients.

## Theory & Experiments

### Theory — assuming feature shift and analyzing convergence

The theory section models feature shift mathematically through **Assumption 4.1**. The key point is that each client $i$'s input features may have a different covariance. Setting the mean to zero, the covariance is defined as

$$\mathbb{E}[x_i] = 0, \qquad S_i = \mathbb{E}[x_i x_i^\top]$$

where $S_i$ may differ across clients. That is, it assumes a situation in which **the covariance structure of the feature distribution differs across clients**, rather than the label distribution. The paper states this explicitly as "local feature distributions differ — not label distribution."

Under this assumption, the convergence rates of FedAvg and FedBN are compared. However, the analysis is carried out not for general deep networks but in a simplified setting: a **two-layer ReLU network, squared loss, gradient descent, an over-parameterized regime, and local update $E=1$**. From an NTK / Gram matrix perspective, it shows that FedBN's Gram matrix can have a more favorable smallest eigenvalue, and consequently argues that FedBN can achieve a faster convergence rate than FedAvg.

### Experiments — constructing the feature-shift setting directly

The experiments do more than assert that feature shift exists; they construct settings where each client's feature domain genuinely differs. The main benchmark uses five digit datasets.

- SVHN
- USPS
- SynthDigits
- MNIST-M
- MNIST

All five are digit classification tasks, but their image appearances differ. In other words, the label spaces are similar while the input feature distributions differ — a canonical feature-shift setting. The paper treats each dataset as a distinct domain and assigns each client a single domain. As a result, the differences across clients resemble **differences in image style, background, contrast, and appearance** rather than label skew. It also varies the number of clients per dataset to sweep the level of feature heterogeneity while comparing FedBN and FedAvg.

Beyond the benchmark, the paper uses real-world datasets in which feature shift arises naturally.

- **Office-Caltech10**: different capture environments and camera sources such as Amazon, Caltech, DSLR, and Webcam.
- **DomainNet**: different image styles such as Clipart, Infograph, Painting, Quickdraw, Real, and Sketch.
- **ABIDE**: fMRI data collected from different medical institutions. Differences in equipment and protocol across sites make it a medical FL example of feature shift.

Here each source or institution is treated as a client, creating a setting where feature distributions differ across clients. Across all of these feature-shift non-IID settings, FedBN outperforms both FedAvg and FedProx.

## Takeaways

By adjusting what FedAvg aggregates, FedBN turns BN layers into a client-specific adaptation module for non-IID FL under strong feature shift — a simple yet powerful approach. It is also one of the representative early works to actively bring BN into federated learning.

That said, it is worth distinguishing a few things when reading the results. What the paper does and does not do can be summarized as follows.

**What it does**

- Explicitly defines feature shift and formalizes the theoretical setting through Assumption 4.1.
- Shows via a toy example that BN can harmonize the loss surface.
- Constructs distinct per-client domains in the benchmark and validates performance on real-world multi-domain/multi-site datasets.

**What it does not do**

- It does not rigorously estimate $P_i(x)$ and $P_i(x \mid y)$ for every real-world dataset to statistically prove feature shift.
- It only partially quantifies the feature distribution distance between clients to directly measure "how much feature shift there is."
- The theoretical proof is not a complete proof for general CNNs, but is limited to a simplified over-parameterized two-layer ReLU setting.

Despite these limitations, by responding to the feature-shift problem with the minimal intervention of deciding "what not to average," FedBN became a concise baseline that later personalized FL work continues to reference.
