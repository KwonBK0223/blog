> **Paper**: DeepSurv: Personalized Treatment Recommender System Using a Cox Proportional Hazards Deep Neural Network — Jared L. Katzman, Uri Shaham, Alexander Cloninger, Jonathan Bates, Tingting Jiang, Yuval Kluger, *BMC Medical Research Methodology* 18, 24 (2018). [DOI:10.1186/s12874-018-0482-1](https://doi.org/10.1186/s12874-018-0482-1)

## TL;DR

DeepSurv is a survival model that predicts the log-risk function of the Cox proportional hazards (CoxPH) model with a deep neural network instead of a linear combination $\beta^\top x$. The training objective is the negative log of the Cox partial likelihood, augmented with L2 regularization and dropout. When a categorical variable encoding the treatment group is included among the inputs, the network learns interactions between covariates and treatment, and comparing the risks obtained by passing the same patient through the network under different treatment conditions yields a patient-specific treatment recommendation. DeepSurv matches CoxPH on linear data and outperforms it on nonlinear data, and it has since become the standard baseline for deep learning based survival analysis.

## Background

### The structure of survival data

Survival data consists of three components per patient.

- **Baseline covariates $x$**: predictors such as age, sex, disease stage, laboratory values, and treatment information
- **Observed time $T$**: either the time at which the event occurred or the time of last follow-up
- **Event indicator $E$**: $E = 1$ if the event (death, recurrence, and so on) was actually observed, $E = 0$ under **right censoring**

Right censoring means that no event had occurred up to the last follow-up and the subsequent outcome is unknown. In that case $T$ does not record the event time but only the partial information that no event occurred at least until $T$. Discarding censored samples as one would in ordinary regression, or treating the observed time as if it were the event time, introduces serious bias. This is the fundamental reason survival models are needed.

### Survival function and hazard function

Let the event time be a random variable $T$. The survival function is the probability that no event has occurred up to a given time.

$$
S(t) = P(T \ge t)
$$

The hazard function $h(t)$ is the instantaneous risk of the event occurring in the next infinitesimal interval, conditional on having survived event-free up to time $t$.

$$
h(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{P(t \le T < t + \Delta t \mid T \ge t)}{\Delta t}
$$

A high hazard means that among the patients who have survived to that point, the immediate death or failure risk is high. The two functions are linked through the cumulative hazard.

$$
S(t) = \exp\left(-\int_0^t h(u)\, du\right)
$$

### CoxPH and its linearity assumption

CoxPH factorizes the hazard into a time-dependent baseline hazard and a patient-specific risk term.

$$
h(t \mid x) = h_0(t) \exp\big(\beta^\top x\big)
$$

Here $h_0(t)$ is the baseline risk shared by all patients, and $\beta^\top x$ is the **log-risk function**, the patient-specific risk determined by the covariates on a log scale. The central assumption of CoxPH is that this log-risk is a **linear combination** of the covariates, that is, the linear proportional hazards condition.

This assumption allows each coefficient $\beta_i$ to be read directly as a hazard ratio, which is why CoxPH became the standard tool of clinical statistics. In real clinical data, however, nonlinear effects and interactions among variables are common, and the linear assumption is an overly strong restriction. Various nonlinear alternatives have been proposed to relax it, including classification-based methods, time-encoded methods, and risk-predicting methods, and neural networks had been tried as well, but earlier neural survival models failed to consistently outperform CoxPH. DeepSurv targets exactly this gap.

## Method

### The Cox partial likelihood loss

DeepSurv replaces the linear log-risk $\beta^\top x$ of CoxPH with a neural network $\hat{h}_\theta(x)$, so that

$$
h(t \mid x) = h_0(t) \exp\big(\hat{h}_\theta(x)\big)
$$

where $\theta$ denotes the network weights. What training requires is not the baseline hazard $h_0(t)$ but the relative risk among patients, so the Cox partial likelihood is used directly.

$$
L_c(\theta) = \prod_{i:\, E_i = 1} \frac{\exp\big(\hat{h}_\theta(x_i)\big)}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{R}(T_i)} \exp\big(\hat{h}_\theta(x_j)\big)}
$$

Here $\mathcal{R}(T_i) = \{ j : T_j \ge T_i \}$ is the **risk set** at time $T_i$, the set of patients still event-free at that moment. Censored patients ($E = 0$) contribute nothing to the numerator, but they remain members of the risk set for as long as they were observed and therefore contribute to the denominator. This is precisely where the censoring information is used explicitly rather than discarded.

The loss is the negative log of this partial likelihood plus an L2 penalty.

$$
\ell(\theta) = -\sum_{i:\, E_i = 1} \left[ \hat{h}_\theta(x_i) - \log \sum_{j \in \mathcal{R}(T_i)} \exp\big(\hat{h}_\theta(x_j)\big) \right] + \lambda \lVert \theta \rVert_2^2
$$

The advantage of the partial likelihood is that $\hat{h}_\theta$ can be learned without ever estimating the baseline hazard, and since this loss is differentiable with respect to the network parameters it can be optimized directly by backpropagation.

### Architecture and regularization

The network itself is straightforward.

- **Input**: the patient's baseline covariates. In the treatment experiments, a categorical variable encoding the treatment group is supplied alongside them.
- **Hidden layers**: several fully connected layers with ReLU or SELU activations
- **Regularization**: dropout, L2 weight decay, batch normalization
- **Output**: a single node giving the predicted log-risk $\hat{h}_\theta(x)$
- **Optimization**: Adam or momentum-based SGD, learning-rate decay, gradient clipping
- **Hyperparameter search**: random search over depth, layer width, dropout rate, and regularization strength

The architecture is not elaborate, but every standard safeguard against overfitting is present. The source of the performance lies less in architectural sophistication than in the design choice of using the Cox partial likelihood directly as the loss.

### Learning covariate–treatment interactions

The second axis of DeepSurv is the modeling of treatment effects. When a categorical treatment variable $\tau \in \{0, 1, \dots\}$ is included in the input along with the patient's covariates, the network learns $\hat{h}_\theta(x, \tau)$. In linear CoxPH, capturing an interaction requires a human to hand-design and insert the appropriate cross terms; a neural network, by contrast, can learn complex relationships between covariates and treatment through its hidden layers without any such explicit specification. In effect, it learns from the data which patient profiles make a given treatment more dangerous.

## Experiments

The primary evaluation metric is the **concordance index (C-index)**, a ranking-based measure of whether the model assigns a higher risk to the patient who actually experienced the event sooner when two patients are compared. A value of 0.5 corresponds to random ranking and 1.0 to perfect ranking.

### Simulated data

The authors generate simulated data with a known log-risk function to test whether the model can recover that structure.

- **Linear hazard**: the log-risk is a linear combination of the covariates
- **Nonlinear (Gaussian) hazard**: the log-risk is a nonlinear function of the covariates

On the linear data, DeepSurv performs comparably to CoxPH, confirming that the nonlinear model does not destroy a linear structure by overfitting it. On the nonlinear data, CoxPH fails to capture the structure while DeepSurv recovers the true hazard and clearly outperforms it.

### Real clinical data

The real clinical datasets are WHAS (a myocardial infarction cohort), SUPPORT (seriously ill hospitalized patients), and METABRIC (breast cancer gene expression data). The baselines are CoxPH and Random Survival Forest (RSF), and DeepSurv achieves a C-index competitive with or better than CoxPH across all three datasets.

### Treatment experiments

To validate the modeling of treatment interactions, the authors use simulated data in which the treatment effect varies with the covariates, together with the real Rotterdam and GBSG breast cancer datasets. Training on Rotterdam and evaluating on GBSG tests whether the model successfully captures the risk associated with treatment.

## Treatment Recommendation

The applied contribution of DeepSurv is the use of the trained model as a personalized treatment recommender.

### The recommender function

Given treatments $i$ and $j$, the recommender function is defined as the difference in log-risk obtained by passing the same patient $x$ through the network under each condition.

$$
\text{rec}_{ij}(x) = \hat{h}_\theta(x, i) - \hat{h}_\theta(x, j)
$$

If this value is positive, treatment $i$ carries a higher risk than treatment $j$ for patient $x$, so treatment $j$ is recommended; if it is negative, the reverse holds. The rule is simply to **recommend the treatment that yields the lower predicted risk**. Note that the baseline hazard $h_0(t)$ cancels between the two terms and therefore never needs to be estimated.

### Validating the recommendations

The recommendations are validated by the following procedure.

1. Partition the test-set patients into two groups by comparing the treatment they actually received with the model's recommendation.
   - **Recommendation group**: the treatment received matches the model's recommendation
   - **Anti-recommendation group**: the treatment received contradicts the model's recommendation
2. Compare the Kaplan-Meier survival curves of the two groups.
3. Compare the median survival times of the two groups.
4. Test whether the survival difference between the groups is statistically significant using the log-rank test.

In the experiments, the recommendation group exhibited a longer median survival time than the anti-recommendation group, and the difference was significant under the log-rank test. This suggests the possibility that following the model's recommendations could increase survival time across a patient population.

## Takeaways

- DeepSurv replaces the linear log-risk of CoxPH with a neural network and uses the negative log Cox partial likelihood as its loss, making it a representative deep survival model that handles censoring explicitly while learning nonlinear risk structure.
- The practical contribution of the paper is that a design as simple as including the treatment group as a categorical input suffices to learn covariate–treatment interactions, and that this is extended into a recommender function that produces personalized treatment recommendations.
- That said, **the proportional hazards assumption remains intact.** The strong assumption that hazard ratios do not vary over time is still in place; what has been made nonlinear is the log-risk function, not the proportional hazards structure itself.
- **Interpretability is lacking.** Unlike CoxPH, whose coefficients can be read directly as hazard ratios, the log-risk function of DeepSurv is a black box, which remains an obstacle to clinical adoption. Subsequent work such as CoxKAN targets exactly this weakness.
- **Validation relies heavily on simulation.** The evidence for nonlinear hazard recovery rests largely on simulated data designed by the authors themselves, and it is difficult to verify what nonlinear structure was actually learned on real data.
- **The treatment recommendations should not be read as causal claims.** The validation is a retrospective comparison on observational data rather than a randomized intervention, so it cannot distinguish whether the longer survival of the recommendation group is an effect of the recommendation or an artifact of confounding. Prospective validation would be required before clinical deployment.
