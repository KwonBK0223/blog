## TL;DR

CartNet (short for "A Cartesian Encoding Graph Neural Network") is a graph neural network that **encodes the atomic geometry of a crystal in Cartesian coordinates**, takes the crystal temperature as an additional input, and efficiently predicts crystal properties. It represents a crystal with atomic positions, distances, and directions alone — no unit cell vectors — and combines neighbour equalization to compensate for information loss at longer distances, a Cholesky-based head that guarantees physically valid anisotropic displacement parameters (ADPs), and SO(3) data augmentation for rotational robustness. On an ADP dataset curated from more than 200,000 experimental structures in the Cambridge Structural Database (CSD), it improves ADP prediction over prior methods by 10.87%.

## Background

### What are ADPs?

**ADPs (Anisotropic Displacement Parameters)** describe the extent of thermal vibration of atoms within a crystalline (solid) structure as a $3 \times 3$ covariance matrix. This matrix must be **positive definite**, and it expresses the atom's "thermal ellipsoid" — in which directions and how strongly the atom vibrates. ADPs matter for precise interpretation and prediction of material properties, particularly thermal stability and crystal deformation.

### The problem with the conventional approach (DFT)

ADPs are traditionally obtained from density functional theory (DFT) calculations, which are computationally expensive ($O(N^3)$ or worse) and can take days to weeks as crystal structures grow more complex. This makes fast, large-scale screening over big crystal datasets impractical. Replacing DFT with machine learning — GNNs in particular — allows models trained on experimental and simulation data to predict at extremely high speed, with respectable accuracy and no explicit physics computation.

## Method

CartNet makes four key contributions.

### 1) Cartesian Encoding

Existing crystal property models often require unit cell information or elaborate handling of periodic boundary conditions (PBC). CartNet captures the crystal with only the following minimal geometric information.

1. **Atomic position $p_i$**: the 3D coordinates of each atom
2. **Interatomic distance $d_{ij}$**: the Euclidean distance between atoms $i$ and $j$
3. **Direction vector $v_{ij}$**: the unit vector pointing from $i$ to $j$
4. (Optional) a radial basis function (RBF) embedding to expand distance information into multiple dimensions

Instead of chemical bond information, the graph is built as a **radius graph** (e.g., connecting neighbouring atoms within 5 Å), linking every atom within the radius regardless of bonding. Node features are the atomic number $Z$ and, for ADP prediction, the temperature. This way, spatial relations can be learned without any unit cell information, data preparation is simplified, and diverse crystal structures can be handled consistently.

### 2) Neighbour Equalization

In message-passing GNNs, information from distant atoms can be sharply diluted to the point of being ignored. When long-range interactions such as hydrogen bonds and van der Waals forces matter in a crystal, this hurts accuracy. To prevent it, CartNet applies an **envelope function** whose message weights decay smoothly as the distance $d_{ij}$ grows, and a distance-based gate balances how strongly information propagates. Rather than a hard cutoff ("far means weight zero"), the smooth decay lets close neighbours dominate while distant neighbours never vanish entirely, so long-range interactions are properly reflected.

### 3) Cholesky-based Head

An ADP is a covariance matrix that must be positive definite. Regressing a $3 \times 3$ matrix directly can produce negative eigenvalues, which is physically meaningless. CartNet instead has the network output a lower-triangular matrix $L$ and reconstructs the matrix in Cholesky form.

$$
U = L L^{\top}
$$

Constructed this way, $U$ is automatically symmetric and guaranteed positive definite. Since the network only needs to predict $L$, the mathematical constraint becomes trivial, and the resulting ADPs are physically valid with no negative eigenvalues.

### 4) SO(3) Data Augmentation

Crystal structures should behave equivariantly under 3D rotation, but if the training data is biased toward particular orientations, the model may fail on rotated structures. During training, crystal structures (atomic coordinates) are therefore randomly rotated and included as additional samples. Exposed to inputs at diverse orientations, the model becomes robust to rotation and responds consistently to experimental structures measured at arbitrary angles.

## Experiments

### ADP prediction

The model was trained and evaluated on an ADP dataset curated from more than 200,000 experimental crystal structures in the Cambridge Structural Database (CSD). CartNet outperformed prior methods on ADP prediction by **10.87%** while drastically cutting computational cost. The core achievement is maintaining and improving accuracy while eliminating the enormous time DFT-based calculation demands.

### Extension to other properties

Although designed with ADPs as the target, CartNet transfers well to other crystal property prediction tasks. It also performs strongly on formation energy and band gap prediction on datasets such as Jarvis and the Materials Project, demonstrating its potential as a general-purpose crystal property model rather than a single-task specialist.

## Takeaways

- Encoding crystals with Cartesian coordinates, distances, and directions — **no unit cell and no periodic boundary handling** — simplifies data preparation and treats diverse crystal structures uniformly.
- The **Cholesky head** structurally guarantees the positive definiteness of predicted ADPs, ruling out physically invalid outputs by construction.
- **Neighbour equalization** preserves long-range interactions, and **SO(3) augmentation** provides rotational robustness.
- Starting as an ADP-specific model, it generalizes well to formation energy, band gap, and other properties.

### Limitations

- Cell expansion is not directly modeled.
- Scarce data at extreme temperatures constrains generalization.
- Amorphous materials remain out of reach for now.
