> **Paper**: Distributed cross-learning for equitable federated models — privacy-preserving prediction on data from five California hospitals — Kuo et al., *Nature Communications*, 2025. [DOI: 10.1038/s41467-025-56510-9](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-56510-9)

## TL;DR

- Clinical data is scattered across institutions both **horizontally** and **vertically**, and legal, ethical, and policy constraints make moving raw data essentially impossible.
- D-CLEF is a fully decentralized collaborative learning framework that never exports patient-level data and can train on both horizontally and vertically partitioned data in a peer-to-peer fashion, **with no central server**.
- Training metadata is recorded on a permissioned **blockchain** and model parameters on a **distributed file system (DFS)**, securing auditability and scalability at the same time.
- On three real clinical problems from five California hospitals — COVID-19 mortality prediction, prolonged post-surgical stay prediction, and myocardial infarction prediction — it achieved performance with **no statistically significant difference from centralized learning**.
- This is not a paper proposing a new VFL algorithm or cryptographic guarantees; it is a paper that **demonstrates a training infrastructure (an architecture) that healthcare institutions can actually agree on and operate**.

## Background

### Why healthcare needs collaborative learning

Clinical machine learning is valuable for patient outcome prediction, disease risk scoring, and drug–outcome association analysis, but single-institution data suffers from small samples, unstable learning of rare events such as death or complications, and bias and generalization problems. Combining data across institutions is therefore the fundamental premise.

There are two realistic data-integration scenarios.

- **Horizontal partition**: multiple hospitals share the same variable structure but hold different patients — the setting classic federated learning (FL) has mostly addressed.
- **Vertical partition**: for the same patients, each institution holds different information (encounters/labs, prescriptions, long-term follow-up records, etc.). In clinical reality, this is closer to the norm.

The authors call each institution a **site** rather than a hospital: a site is not merely where data lives, but an autonomous entity that owns its data, performs local computation, and participates in the overall training as an equal.

### Limitations of existing approaches

- **Centralized learning**: the performance gold standard, but moving patient data creates re-identification risks that make it legally, ethically, and politically near-impossible.
- **Federated learning**: keeps data local and improves privacy, but requires a central server. That server becomes a single point of control/failure and leaves the governance question of who owns and operates it. Most FL also supports only horizontal partitions, with limited vertical support.
- **Existing distributed/cryptographic methods**: predominantly horizontal, and ill-suited to real-world clinical collaboration.

The authors define four requirements for an ideal collaborative learning system: (1) **Data** — support both horizontal and vertical integration while never letting patient-level data leave; (2) **Site** — a peer-to-peer structure without a central server, with equitable computational burden; (3) **Process** — performance mathematically equivalent to centralized learning, with a recorded and auditable training process; (4) **Model** — verifiable model provenance and storage for large models. These four requirements are D-CLEF's design philosophy.

## Method

### System architecture: two networks

D-CLEF consists of two permissioned networks.

- **Modeling network (blockchain)**: records the training order, parameter update history, and smart contract executions. Only **metadata** goes on-chain — patient count $n$, covariate count $m$, site information, timestamps/iterations, model type and size, validation results, and the content ID pointing to the actual model. Raw features and even feature names or meanings never leave a site.
- **Storage network (distributed file system)**: stores the compressed model parameters themselves. Only a link (hash) is kept on the blockchain, so auditability and scalability are secured simultaneously.

Each site simultaneously plays three logical roles: a computing node holding local data, a modeling node participating in the blockchain, and a storage node sharing models. In short: **data stays local, the process goes on the blockchain, the artifacts go to distributed storage**.

### Training procedure

D-CLEF has no fixed server. Instead, the aggregator role rotates among sites in **round-robin** fashion — a structural safeguard against any single institution always sitting at the center.

The vertical training flow, based on logistic regression, works as follows. For patient $i$, each site $k$ computes a partial logit using only its own feature block $x_i^{(k)}$.

$$z_i^{(k)} = x_i^{(k)} \cdot w^{(k)}$$

No raw features are transmitted — only this partial logit goes to the current round's aggregator site. The aggregator simply sums them to form the predicted probability.

$$z_i = \sum_{k} z_i^{(k)} + b, \qquad p_i = \sigma(z_i)$$

The common error signal $e_i = p_i - y_i$ is then computed and broadcast to all sites, and each site updates only its own parameters locally.

$$\nabla w^{(k)} = \sum_i e_i \, x_i^{(k)}, \qquad w^{(k)} \leftarrow w^{(k)} - \eta \nabla w^{(k)}$$

This update is **mathematically identical** to gradient descent for centralized logistic regression. No site ever learns another site's features, and in the horizontal scenario the same skeleton aggregates local gradient summaries instead of partial logits. At the end of each iteration, the updated parameters are stored on the DFS, a new model hash is recorded on the blockchain, and the next aggregator is designated.

## Experiments

Three real clinical datasets were used for validation.

- **Dataset X — UC Health COVID-19 mortality prediction**: $n = 15{,}279$ confirmed COVID-19 patients from five University of California (UC) hospitals, $m = 100$ covariates (10 patient attributes, 6 COVID-related, 84 prescribed medications). For the vertical scenario, covariates were split into two blocks: (patient + COVID information, 16) and (medications, 84).
- **Dataset Y — prolonged stay (3+ days) after total hip arthroplasty (THA)**: $n = 960$, $m = 34$. A horizontal experiment simulated two hospitals via stratified patient splitting, and a vertical experiment split covariates into three blocks.
- **Dataset Z — Edinburgh myocardial infarction public dataset**: $n = 1{,}253$, $m = 9$ (5 symptom + 4 ECG features). Splitting symptoms vs. ECG makes the vertical configuration natural here.

The baselines are (1) **siloed learning**, where each hospital trains only on its own data, (2) **centralized learning** with all data pooled (the theoretical upper bound), and (3) **D-CLEF** (horizontal/vertical).

The key result is consistent: across all hospitals, **siloed < D-CLEF ≈ centralized**, with no statistically significant difference between D-CLEF and centralized learning. In other words, the decentralized architecture imposes no performance penalty — the operational structure can be changed at no cost in accuracy. In a temporal validation of whether a model trained on a small early cohort holds up on a larger subsequent cohort, D-CLEF again closely approximated centralized learning with small per-hospital variance. The one cost: synchronization over the blockchain and DFS increases wall-clock time relative to conventional FL.

## Takeaways

It matters to separate what this paper proves from what it deliberately does not attempt.

**What it proves**
- Without a central server, performance equivalent to centralized learning is achievable in both horizontal and vertical settings.
- Performance stays stable as institutions are added and data distributions shift, and no single institution disproportionately benefits.
- All of this is demonstrated on real multi-institutional clinical data.

**What it does not do**
- Propose a new VFL algorithm, prove cryptographic privacy guarantees, benchmark deep learning models, or solve the patient matching problem.

The limitations are equally clear. The model is logistic regression; feature engineering and patient matching are assumed solved in advance; and the communication cost of blockchain + DFS could burden real-time clinical settings. The threat model is honest-but-curious — passive adversaries who follow the protocol faithfully but try to infer other participants' data from shared information — and malicious attacks are out of scope.

Even so, the paper's value is unmistakable. It identifies the real bottleneck of clinical collaboration as **governance and trust**, not algorithms, and offers a systemic answer to "who built the model, and when, on what scope of data?" It built not the fastest FL, but **a training infrastructure that healthcare institutions can actually agree on and operate** — which is why D-CLEF is an architecture paper, not an algorithm paper.
