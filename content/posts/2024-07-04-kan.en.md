> **Paper**: KAN: Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks — Ziming Liu, Yixuan Wang, Sachin Vaidya, Fabian Ruehle, James Halverson, Marin Soljačić, Thomas Y. Hou, Max Tegmark, 2024. [arXiv:2404.19756](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.19756)

## TL;DR

- Where MLPs place **fixed activation functions on nodes** and learnable linear weights on edges, KANs place **learnable activation functions on edges** and compute only sums at the nodes. There are no linear weight matrices at all — every weight is replaced by a univariate function parameterized as a B-spline.
- The fixed $(n, 2n+1, 1)$ structure of the Kolmogorov–Arnold representation theorem is generalized to arbitrary widths and depths, so KANs can be stacked like ordinary neural networks.
- On function approximation tasks, KANs exhibit **faster neural scaling laws** than MLPs, achieving lower error at the same parameter count.
- Through sparsification, pruning, and symbolification, a trained network can be **reduced to a symbolic formula**, giving strong interpretability, and the locality of splines mitigates catastrophic forgetting in continual learning.

## Background

### The Problem with MLPs

MLPs (fully connected layers + activation functions) are the most fundamental building block of deep learning, but they have two problems. First, **interpretability** — they are essentially black boxes, hard to understand without post-analysis tools. Second, **representational efficiency** — while the universal approximation theorem guarantees they can represent any function in theory, better parameter efficiency is needed in practice.

### Kolmogorov–Arnold Representation Theorem

If $f$ is a multivariate continuous function on a bounded domain, then $f$ can be written as a finite composition of univariate continuous functions and the binary operation of addition. For smooth $f: [0,1]^n \to \mathbb{R}$,

$$f(x) = f(x_1, \dots, x_n) = \sum_{q=1}^{2n+1} \Phi_q\!\left( \sum_{p=1}^{n} \phi_{q,p}(x_p) \right), \quad \phi_{q,p}: [0,1] \to \mathbb{R},\; \Phi_q: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$$

This means learning a high-dimensional function reduces, in essence, to learning one-dimensional functions. However, the univariate functions in the theorem can in general be non-smooth or even fractal, so the result had long been considered impractical for machine learning. The authors shift perspective in two ways: (1) rather than being bound to the fixed structure of width $2n+1$ and depth 2, they **generalize to arbitrary widths and depths**, and (2) they rely on the fact that functions arising in science and everyday life are usually smooth and therefore well approximated by splines.

Another piece of background is the complementarity of splines and MLPs. Splines are accurate in low dimensions but suffer severely from the **curse of dimensionality (COD)**, while MLPs are relatively robust to COD but less accurate than splines in low dimensions. KANs aim for a combination that learns features like an MLP on the outside and optimizes to high precision like a spline on the inside.

## Method

### Architecture: Learnable Activation Functions on Edges

The contrast between MLPs and KANs can be summarized as follows.

$$\text{MLP}(x) = (W_3 \circ \sigma_2 \circ W_2 \circ \sigma_1 \circ W_1)(x)$$
$$\text{KAN}(x) = (\Phi_3 \circ \Phi_2 \circ \Phi_1)(x)$$

An MLP alternates learnable linear transformations with fixed nonlinear activations, whereas a KAN composes **only learnable nonlinear transformations $\Phi_l$**. Each $\Phi_l$ is a matrix of univariate activation functions; input values pass directly through the edge activation functions — with no linear transformation — and are then summed at the nodes. Every edge (connection) has its own distinct activation function.

Each activation function is parameterized as the sum of a basis function and a spline.

$$\phi(x) = \omega_b\, b(x) + \omega_s\, \text{spline}(x)$$

$$b(x) = \text{silu}(x) = \frac{x}{1 + e^{-x}}, \qquad \text{spline}(x) = \sum_i c_i B_i(x)$$

The $B_i$ are B-spline (basis spline) basis functions, and the coefficients $c_i$ are the trainable weights of the KAN. Using more basis functions increases expressiveness.

### Approximation Theory and Scaling

**Theorem 2.1 (KAT)**: if $f$ is representable as $f = (\Phi_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ \Phi_0)(x)$ and each $\Phi_{l,i,j}$ is $(k+1)$-times continuously differentiable, then for a KAN built from $k$-th order B-splines with grid size $G$,

$$\| f - (\Phi^G_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ \Phi^G_0) x \|_{C^m} \le C\, G^{-k-1+m}$$

This bound is **independent of the dimension $n$** — showing that for functions with such compositional structure, the curse of dimensionality can be overcome. One can increase $k$ for smoothness, but making it too large can cause oscillations that hinder optimization.

### Grid Extension

**Grid extension** — progressively refining the spline grid and carrying over the B-spline coefficients — lets accuracy be improved in stages without retraining the whole model from scratch. Training starts on a coarse grid and refines gradually; more grid points are not unconditionally better (test performance degrades past the interpolation threshold), and an optimal grid exists for a given data scale.

### A Simplification Pipeline for Interpretability

1. **Sparsification**: since KANs have no linear weights, L1 + entropy regularization is applied to the activation functions.
2. **Pruning**: unimportant neurons are removed to minimize parameters.
3. **Symbolification**: the remaining activation functions are snapped to expected symbolic functions such as $\cos$, $\log$, or $x^2$, the affine parameters are retrained, and the network finally **outputs a symbolic formula**.

Unlike conventional symbolic regression, which is intractable due to its search space and hard to debug, KANs layer symbolification on top of continuous training, making the process interactive.

## Experiments

- **Toy function approximation**: on $f(x,y) = \exp(\sin(\pi x) + y^2)$, $xy$, a 100-dimensional example, and others, KAN's test RMSE decreases close to the theoretical $N^{-4}$ scaling, consistently outperforming MLPs (depth 2–5) at the same parameter count.
- **Special functions**: on 15 special functions including Bessel functions, elliptic integrals, and spherical harmonics, KANs form a more parameter-efficient and accurate Pareto frontier than MLPs.
- **Feynman datasets**: on physics-formula regression tasks, even without knowing the KAN shape in advance, pruning discovers small KANs close to human-constructed shapes, with strong performance.
- **Solving PDEs**: on the 2D Poisson equation, a KAN [2,10,1] achieves lower L2/H1 error than a much larger MLP [2,100,100,100,1].
- **Continual learning**: in a task of sequentially learning five Gaussian peaks, MLPs forget previous regions when learning a new one, whereas KANs preserve the past. Because the **spline basis is local**, a new sample affects only a few nearby coefficients while distant coefficients remain intact.
- **AI + Science applications**: rediscovering relationships among topological invariants in knot theory, extracting the mobility edge in Anderson localization in physics — demonstrating a "collaborator" role in (re)discovering laws while interacting with scientists.

## Takeaways

**Strengths**
1. **Improved expressiveness** and faster scaling from diverse learnable activation functions
2. **Interpretability** via pruning and symbolification
3. Efficiency of spline-based learning — high accuracy with few parameters
4. A wide range of potential applications, centered on scientific problems

**Weaknesses**
1. Implementation is complex and training is slow — typically about 10x slower than an MLP with the same parameter count, which is the main bottleneck.
2. **High computational cost** from per-function spline evaluation — burdensome for real-time or resource-constrained settings
3. Perfect interpretation is still not possible in every situation.
4. Validation on large-scale, complex datasets remains to be done.

The authors' concluding guidance is also practical: choose KANs for functions with compositional structure, tasks requiring high precision, continual learning, and scientific problems needing quantitative interpretation; choose MLPs when fast training is the top priority. Whether KANs will replace MLPs is an open question, but the shift in perspective toward "learning the activation functions" and the simultaneous pursuit of accuracy and interpretability became the starting point for the explosion of KAN-variant research that followed.
