> **Paper**: MLP-Mixer: An All-MLP Architecture for Vision — Ilya Tolstikhin, Neil Houlsby, Alexander Kolesnikov, Lucas Beyer, Xiaohua Zhai, Thomas Unterthiner, Jessica Yung, Andreas Steiner, Daniel Keysers, Jakob Uszkoreit, Mario Lucic, Alexey Dosovitskiy, NeurIPS 2021. [arXiv:2105.01601](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.01601)

## TL;DR

"While convolutions and attention are both sufficient for good performance, neither of them is necessary." That one sentence summarizes the entire paper. MLP-Mixer slices an image into patches to form a token sequence, then simply alternates a **token-mixing MLP** (learning spatial relations across patches) with a **channel-mixing MLP** (learning feature combinations within each patch) — and reaches performance competitive with contemporary SOTA on ImageNet classification. The simplicity buys substantial throughput and ease of implementation, and it revives the fundamental architecture-design question: what do we actually need?

## Background

Two lineages dominated image recognition architectures.

- **CNNs**: bake strong inductive biases — locality and translation equivariance — into local filters. Data-efficient, but the receptive field is local.
- **Transformers (ViT)**: model global interactions directly via self-attention. Highly expressive, but attention is heavy and its complexity grows with the number of tokens.

So how far can pure MLPs go, with neither local convolution nor global attention? MLP-Mixer is an empirical answer to that question.

## Method

### Input: patches and channels

The image is cut into non-overlapping patches (tokens), and each patch is linearly projected (per-patch fully-connected) into a $C$-dimensional vector. With $S$ patches and $C$ channels, the input becomes a table $X \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times C}$.

- **Patch (= Token)**: a small crop of the image, the unit that lets an image be treated as a sequence. It tokenizes the input without any convolution.
- **Channel**: the feature dimension after each patch is embedded.

### Mixer Layer: two kinds of MLP

A single Mixer layer consists of two stages. Both use LayerNorm and skip connections, and each MLP is a Fully-connected → GELU → Fully-connected block.

**1) Token-mixing MLP** — fixes a channel and applies an MLP along the patch axis. Operating column-wise, it learns interactions across spatial locations, i.e., how much each patch relates to the others.

$$U_{*,i} = X_{*,i} + W_2\,\sigma\!\left(W_1\, \mathrm{LayerNorm}(X)_{*,i}\right), \quad i = 1 \dots C$$

**2) Channel-mixing MLP** — fixes a patch and applies an MLP along the channel axis. Operating row-wise, it learns combinations among the features inside each patch, transforming the patch into a richer representation.

$$Y_{j,*} = U_{j,*} + W_4\,\sigma\!\left(W_3\, \mathrm{LayerNorm}(U)_{j,*}\right), \quad j = 1 \dots S$$

The same token-mixing MLP is shared across all patches and the same channel-mixing MLP across all channel positions, so the parameter count grows only modestly with input size. After stacking $N$ Mixer layers, Global Average Pooling and a fully-connected classification head produce the prediction. Unlike ViT, no explicit position embeddings are used — the token-mixing MLP can learn location-dependent weights on its own.

From another angle, token-mixing can be viewed as a "parameter-shared depthwise convolution whose kernel covers the entire spatial extent," and channel-mixing as a "1×1 convolution." In other words, Mixer is less a degenerate CNN than a structure that separates the two operations — spatial mixing and channel mixing — into their simplest possible forms.

## Experiments

The key results come from the large-scale pre-training regime.

| Model | Family | ImNet top-1 | Throughput (img/sec/core) | TPUv3 core-days |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mixer-L/16 | MLP | 84.15 | 105 | 0.41k |
| ViT-L/16 | Attention | 85.30 | 32 | 0.18k |
| BiT-R152x4 | CNN | 85.39 | 26 | 0.94k |
| HaloNet | Attention | 85.8 | 120 | 0.10k |

(Pre-trained on ImageNet-21k)

- Mixer-L/16 is slightly less accurate than ViT-L/16 but delivers **more than 3× the throughput**.
- Mixer-H/14 pre-trained on the proprietary JFT-300M reaches 87.94% top-1, surpassing BiT-R152x4 and narrowing the gap to ViT-H/14 to about 0.6 points.
- Overall, Mixer's performance climbs steeply as data scales up — consistent with the ViT lesson that architectures with weaker inductive biases benefit most from large-scale data.

## Takeaways

- A radical attempt to build everything out of MLPs. No convolution, no attention. Simply alternating token-mixing and channel-mixing handles both spatial relations across patches and feature relations across channels.
- It approaches CNN/Transformer performance while offering clear advantages in throughput, compute efficiency, and ease of implementation.
- It leaves a question about the essence of architecture design: if the source of performance is not attention as a specific operation but the function of "mixing information across tokens," then the core of design becomes finding the cheapest structure that performs that function.
- The idea spread quickly beyond vision. In time-series forecasting, **TSMixer** (Chen et al., TMLR 2023) handles temporal dependencies with time-mixing and feature-mixing MLPs, and the **PatchMixer family** followed along the patch-based direction. "Cut into patches, mix along two axes" has become a domain-agnostic design grammar.
